## 📖 Concept Overview

### What Are Percentages?

A percentage is simply a fraction with a denominator of 100. The word "percent" comes from Latin *per centum* — "by the hundred." So 45% means 45 out of every 100 parts.

**The Core Insight**: Everything in percentages reduces to one simple idea — work with base 100, then scale.

### 1. Basic Percentage Conversion

**Definition**: A percentage x% = x/100. It can be converted to a fraction or decimal and vice versa.

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Think of it as a calculator's display. 1/4 on a calculator shows as 0.25, which is 25/100 = 25%.

**Essential Conversions to Memorize**

| Percentage | Fraction | Decimal |
|---|---|---|
| 1% | 1/100 | 0.01 |
| 5% | 1/20 | 0.05 |
| 10% | 1/10 | 0.1 |
| 12.5% | 1/8 | 0.125 |
| 16.67% | 1/6 | 0.1666 |
| 20% | 1/5 | 0.2 |
| 25% | 1/4 | 0.25 |
| 33.33% | 1/3 | 0.3333 |
| 37.5% | 3/8 | 0.375 |
| 40% | 2/5 | 0.4 |
| 50% | 1/2 | 0.5 |
| 62.5% | 5/8 | 0.625 |
| 66.67% | 2/3 | 0.6667 |
| 75% | 3/4 | 0.75 |
| 80% | 4/5 | 0.8 |
| 87.5% | 7/8 | 0.875 |
| 100% | 1 | 1.0 |
| 125% | 5/4 | 1.25 |
| 150% | 3/2 | 1.5 |
| 200% | 2 | 2.0 |

> [!NOTE]
> **Memory Trick**
> Learn the 1/8 family first (12.5%, 25%, 37.5%, 50%, 62.5%, 75%, 87.5%) — they're the most frequently used in DI and profit/loss questions.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students forget that percentages greater than 100% are valid. 150% means 1.5 times the original quantity.

### 2. Finding Percentage of a Number

**Formula**: x% of N = (x/100) × N

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Move the decimal two places left for 1%, then multiply.

**Example**: 15% of 240
Method 1: (15/100) × 240 = 0.15 × 240 = 36
Method 2 (fast): 10% = 24, 5% = 12, 15% = 24 + 12 = 36
Method 3 (fastest): 15% = 3/20 → 240/20 × 3 = 12 × 3 = 36

**Shortcut — The 10% Split**:
Break any percentage into multiples of 10% and 5%, which are easy to calculate mentally.
- 35% = 3×10% + 5%
- 27% = 2×10% + 5% + 2×1%
- 15% = 10% + 5%

**The Commutative Trick** (most powerful shortcut):
x% of y = y% of x
This means you can swap to whichever is easier to calculate.

Examples:
- 18% of 50 = 50% of 18 = 9 ✓
- 8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = 2 ✓
- 4% of 75 = 75% of 4 = 3 ✓
- 6% of 150 = 150% of 6 = 9 ✓

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students calculate 4% of 75 by doing 75 × 0.04 = 3.0. When they could instantly see 75% of 4 = 3.

### 3. Finding What Percentage One Number Is of Another

**Formula**: (Part / Whole) × 100 = Percentage

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Always divide the smaller by the larger when finding "what percent of A is B?" The base (A) goes in the denominator.

**Standard Phrase Translation**:

| Phrase | What It Means |
|---|---|
| "A is what % of B?" | (A / B) × 100 |
| "What % of A is B?" | (B / A) × 100 |
| "A is x% more than B" | A = B × (1 + x/100) |
| "A is x% less than B" | A = B × (1 - x/100) |
| "% increase from A to B" | [(B-A)/A] × 100 |
| "% decrease from A to B" | [(A-B)/A] × 100 |

**Example**: If the price of a shirt goes from ₹400 to ₹500, by what percent did it increase?
% increase = (500-400)/400 × 100 = 100/400 × 100 = 25%

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Using the new value as the denominator instead of the original. % increase always has the **original** value as base.

### 4. Percentage Increase & Decrease

**Increase Formula**: New = Original × (1 + r/100)
**Decrease Formula**: New = Original × (1 - r/100)

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Think of it like a multiplier. 1.25 means 125% = 25% increase from base.

**Key Relationship**:
If A increases by r%, it becomes A(1 + r/100).
If it then decreases by r%, it does NOT return to original.
Example: ₹100 → 20% increase → ₹120 → 20% decrease → ₹96. Not ₹100.

**The Symmetry Trick**:
- If something increases by x%, to find the original: divide by (1 + x/100)
- If something decreases by x%, to find the original: divide by (1 - x/100)

**Example**: A car costs ₹5,00,000 after a 25% increase. Original price?
Original = 5,00,000 / 1.25 = ₹4,00,000

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students multiply by (1 - r/100) when finding original after decrease. Should divide, not multiply.

---

### 5. Successive Percentage Change (The Multiplier Method)

**Concept**: When multiple percentage changes happen one after another, use multiplication of multipliers, not addition.

**Formula**: Final = Original × (1 + r₁/100) × (1 + r₂/100) × ...

**Example**: A product's price is increased by 20%, then decreased by 10%.
Final = 100 × 1.20 × 0.90 = 108
Net % change = 8% increase

**Shortcuts for Common Combinations**:
- Increase by x%, then decrease by x%: Net change = -(x²/100)%
  Example: +20%, -20% → -400/100 = -4% (as shown above)
- Increase by a% and b%: Net ≈ a + b + (ab/100)%
- Decrease by a% and b%: Net ≈ -(a + b) + (ab/100)%
- Increase by a%, decrease by b%: Net = a - b - (ab/100)%

**The +a%, -b% Net Formula**:
Net % change = a - b - (a × b)/100

**Example**: Price increases by 30%, then decreases by 20%.
Net = 30 - 20 - (30×20)/100 = 10 - 6 = **4% increase**

**Exam Hack**: When a value changes by x% and y% successively, the order matters for perception but the multiplier method always gives the correct final value.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Adding percentages directly. 20% increase followed by 20% decrease is NOT 0% — it's a 4% net decrease.

### 6. Reverse Percentage (Finding the Original)

**Concept**: Given the result after a percentage change, find the original value.

**Formula**: Original = New Value / (1 ± r/100)

**Two Types**:

**Type 1 — After Increase**:
Original = New / (1 + r/100)
Example: After 25% increase, price = ₹625. Original = 625/1.25 = ₹500

**Type 2 — After Decrease**:
Original = New / (1 - r/100)
Example: After 20% discount, price = ₹400. Original = 400/0.80 = ₹500

**The Universal Reverse Formula**:
Original × (1 ± r/100) = New
→ Original = New / (1 ± r/100)
→ Multiply numerator and denominator by 100 to clear decimals.

**Example**: 37.5% of a number is 225. Find the number.
37.5% = 3/8
(3/8) × N = 225 → N = 225 × 8/3 = 600

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Forgetting to use the reciprocal multiplier. After a 20% decrease, multiplying by 0.8 gives new, not original. Must divide by 0.8.

### 7. Percentage Points vs Percentage

**Concept**: Percentage points measure absolute difference between two percentages. Percentage measures relative change.

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> If interest rate goes from 8% to 10%, it increased by 2 percentage points but increased by 25% relative to the original rate.

**Formula**:
- Percentage point difference = |Rate₁ - Rate₂|
- Percentage change in rate = [(Rate₂ - Rate₁) / Rate₁] × 100

**Example**:
Old price: ₹100, New price: ₹150
- Price increased by 50% (percentage)
- Price increased by 50 percentage points? No — wrong use.

**Real-World Use**:
- RBI cuts repo rate by 50 basis points = 0.5 percentage points
- "Inflation fell from 8% to 6%" → fell by 2 percentage points, fell by 25% relative

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students confuse "percentage point change" with "percentage change." This is the #1 trap in economic data interpretation questions.

### 8. Population/Growth Problems

**Formula**:
- After n years at r% per annum: P × (1 + r/100)^n
- For decrease: P × (1 - r/100)^n

**The Average Growth Rate**: If population grows from P to Q in n years:
r = [(Q/P)^(1/n) - 1] × 100

**Example**: Population grows from 10,000 to 13,310 in 3 years. Find rate.
13000 = 10000 × (1 + r/100)^3
1.33 = (1 + r/100)^3
1 + r/100 = 1.1 → r = 10%

**Half-Life / Doubling Time (Advanced)**:
- Doubling time at r%: T = 72/r years (Rule of 72)
- For r=8%: doubles in 9 years (72/8 = 9)

**Example**: At 12% per annum, population of a town doubles every 6 years. 2 years ago it was 40,000. What is it now?
Now = 40000 × (1.12)^2 = 40000 × 1.2544 = 50,176

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Using simple percentage growth formula for compound growth problems. Always use (1 + r/100)^n.

### 9. Market Share / Election Problems

**Concept**: Share percentages of parts that together form a whole.

**Formula**:
- Share of A = (A's value / Total) × 100%
- New share after change: recalculate total

**Example**:
Total votes = 50,000. Candidate A gets 35%. After losing 20% of their votes to B, what are new shares?
A's votes = 17500. Loses 20%: loses 3500 to B.
New A = 14000, New total = 50000 (unchanged, just transferred)
New A% = 14000/50000 × 100 = **28%**

**Shortcut for Share Changes**:
If A loses x% of its value to B:
- New A% = (A% - x% × A% / 100)
- New B% = (B% + x% × A% / 100)

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students forget that when A loses votes to B, the total stays the same. They incorrectly adjust the total.

### 10. Discount and Marked Price

**Concept**: Three prices — Cost Price (CP), Marked Price (MP), Selling Price (SP).

**Formulas**:
- Discount % = [(MP - SP) / MP] × 100
- Profit % = [(SP - CP) / CP] × 100
- Loss % = [(CP - SP) / CP] × 100

**The Discount Chain**:
If two successive discounts of a% and b% are offered:
Net discount = a + b - (ab/100)%
Example: 20% and 10% off → 20 + 10 - (200/100) = 28% total discount

**Comparison Trick**:
- "30% off on ₹500" vs "20% off on ₹400": 30% of 500 = 150 off. 20% of 400 = 80 off. First is better.

**When MP is Given as Percentage Above CP**:
If MP is marked at x% above CP and a discount of d% is given:
SP = CP × (1 + x/100) × (1 - d/100)
Profit % = (SP - CP)/CP × 100

**Example**: Cost = ₹200. Marked at 40% above cost. Sold at 20% discount.
MP = 200 × 1.40 = 280
SP = 280 × 0.80 = 224
Profit = (224 - 200)/200 × 100 = **12%**

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students calculate SP directly as CP + x% - d% (e.g., CP + 40% - 20% = 20% profit). This is wrong because the discount applies on MP, not CP.

### Memory Tricks

**The "Fraction-First" Trick**: Convert percentages to fractions mentally
- 75% = 3/4 → always round to 3/4 of the number
- 125% = 5/4 → the number plus 1/4 of itself
- 150% = 3/2 → the number plus half of itself

**The "Near 100" Trick**: For percentages near 100, use (100% ± small%)
- 98% of 250 = (100% - 2%) of 250 = 250 - 5 = 245
- 103% of 250 = (100% + 3%) of 250 = 250 + 7.5 = 257.5

## ⚡ Shortcut Techniques & Time-Saving Tricks

### 1. The 10% Mental Calculation Method

For any percentage, decompose into 10%s and 5%s:

**Example: 35% of 480**
- 10% of 480 = 48
- 5% of 480 = 24
- 35% = 3×10% + 5% + 5% (no, 3×10% = 30%, need 5% more)
- 35% = 48 × 3 + 24 = 144 + 24 = **168**

**For 37.5%**: Use 3/8 directly = 480 ÷ 8 × 3 = 60 × 3 = 180

### 2. The Swap Trick (Most Powerful)

**x% of y = y% of x**

Always calculate the easier side.

| Calculation | Swap To | Result |
|---|---|---|
| 8% of 25 | 25% of 8 | 2 |
| 4% of 75 | 75% of 4 | 3 |
| 6% of 150 | 150% of 6 | 9 |
| 12% of 50 | 50% of 12 | 6 |
| 15% of 80 | 80% of 15 | 12 |

### 3. Base-100 Method

Convert everything to base 100, then scale:

**Example**: If 35% of students passed and 455 passed, how many failed?
35% passed → 35 out of 100
455 passed → each "unit" = 455/35 = 13
Total students = 100 × 13 = 1300
Failed = 1300 - 455 = **845**

### 4. The Difference Method for Percentage Comparison

To find what percent A is of B without division:
- A is what percent of B? → Cross-multiply by a convenient percentage
- Is 24 greater or less than 30% of 80? (30% of 80 = 24) → exactly 30%

### 5. Successive Change Shortcuts

**Increase by x%, then decrease by y%**:
Net = (x - y - xy/100)%

**Special Cases**:
- +x%, -x%: Net change = -(x²/100)%
- +50%, -50%: Net = 0 - 2500/100 = -25% (halves!)
- +20%, -10%: Net = 20 - 10 - 2 = 8% increase

**Three successive changes**:
Net = r₁ + r₂ + r₃ + (r₁r₂ + r₂r₃ + r₃r₁)/100 + (r₁r₂r₃)/10000
(But usually just chain the multipliers: 1.20 × 0.90 × 1.10)

### 6. The 100 minus Method

When finding what percentage does NOT have a certain property:
- If 40% passed, then 60% failed
- If 15% are absent, then 85% are present
- If 30% like product A, then up to 70% don't

### 7. Approximate Percentage from Fraction

Convert difficult percentages to nearby simple fractions:
- 33% ≈ 1/3 → use when you see "approximately one-third"
- 66% ≈ 2/3 → double one-third
- 14% ≈ 1/7 → useful for share problems

### 8. Percentage to Multiplier Table

| Increase | Multiplier | Decrease | Multiplier |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1% | 1.01 | 1% | 0.99 |
| 5% | 1.05 | 5% | 0.95 |
| 10% | 1.10 | 10% | 0.90 |
| 20% | 1.20 | 20% | 0.80 |
| 25% | 1.25 | 25% | 0.75 |
| 33.33% | 1.333 | 33.33% | 0.667 |

Memorize the 1.25, 1.33, 1.5, 0.75, 0.67 multipliers — they appear constantly.

## 🔥 Most Frequently Asked Question Patterns

### Pattern 1: Find Percentage of a Number
**Concept**: Basic percentage calculation
**Difficulty**: Easy
**Approach**: x% of N = (x/100) × N or decompose into 10% + 5%
**Fastest**: Use commutative swap if applicable
**Companies**: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant, Accenture

### Pattern 2: Percentage Increase / Decrease
**Concept**: Finding % change from old to new
**Difficulty**: Easy
**Approach**: (Change / Original) × 100
**Fastest**: Remember to always use original as base
**Companies**: All companies

### Pattern 3: Successive Percentage Change
**Concept**: Two or more percentage changes applied
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: Multiply multipliers, or use net formula
**Fastest**: (1 + r₁/100)(1 + r₂/100)... = final multiplier
**Companies**: TCS, Infosys, Amazon, Goldman

### Pattern 4: Reverse Percentage (Original Value)
**Concept**: Given final value after change, find original
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: Divide by multiplier for increase; divide by (1-r/100) for decrease
**Fastest**: Reciprocal method
**Companies**: TCS, Infosys, Deloitte, Amazon

### Pattern 5: Population / Compound Growth
**Concept**: P × (1 + r/100)^n
**Difficulty**: Medium–Hard
**Approach**: Use formula, or for small years chain multipliers
**Fastest**: For doubling/halving: Rule of 72
**Companies**: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman Sachs, Amazon

### Pattern 6: Discount Problems (Single or Chain)
**Concept**: Successive discounts on marked price
**Difficulty**: Easy–Medium
**Approach**: Net discount = a + b - ab/100
**Fastest**: MP × (1 - d₁/100) × (1 - d₂/100)
**Companies**: All companies

### Pattern 7: Profit & Loss as Percentage
**Concept**: SP, CP, markup, discount chain
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: SP = CP × (1 + profit%) = MP × (1 - discount%)
**Fastest**: Work backwards from known values
**Companies**: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant, Deloitte

### Pattern 8: Finding What Percent of A is B
**Concept**: Relative percentage
**Difficulty**: Easy
**Approach**: (B/A) × 100 — always divide smaller by larger for "what percent of"
**Fastest**: Cross-check with 50%, 25% benchmarks
**Companies**: All companies

### Pattern 9: Percentage Points vs Percentage
**Concept**: Interest rate, inflation, market share changes
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: Separate absolute (pp) from relative (%)
**Fastest**: Identify the phrase "percentage points"
**Companies**: Deloitte, Goldman, Amazon, Microsoft

### Pattern 10: Share Redistribution
**Concept**: When one party's share changes, redistributing to others
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: Calculate absolute values first, then new percentages
**Fastest**: Keep total constant, recalculate
**Companies**: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman

### Pattern 11: Finding New Percentage After Change
**Concept**: Base changes, then find new percentage
**Difficulty**: Medium–Hard
**Approach**: Convert to absolute values, adjust, reconvert to percentage
**Fastest**: Never try to adjust the percentage directly — go to numbers first
**Companies**: Infosys, Amazon, Goldman

### Pattern 12: Expressing Fraction as Percentage
**Concept**: Given a fraction, express as percentage
**Difficulty**: Easy
**Approach**: Multiply fraction by 100
**Fastest**: Know 1/3=33.33%, 1/6=16.67%, 1/7=14.29%, 1/8=12.5%
**Companies**: All companies

### Pattern 13: Error / Approximation in Percentage
**Concept**: Approximate value, find error %
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: % error = [(Approx - Exact) / Exact] × 100
**Fastest**: Use percentage of exact value
**Companies**: Deloitte, Amazon, Microsoft

### Pattern 14: Comparison Using Percentage
**Concept**: Which is greater — A's x% or B's y%?
**Difficulty**: Easy–Medium
**Approach**: Calculate both in absolute terms, or use ratio
**Fastest**: Cross-multiply: Is (x% of A) > (y% of B)? → Is (x × A) > (y × B)?
**Companies**: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant

### Pattern 15: Percentage Above/Below a Value
**Concept**: "A is 25% more than B" → A = 1.25B
**Difficulty**: Easy
**Approach**: More than → multiply by (1 + r/100); Less than → multiply by (1 - r/100)
**Fastest**: Direct formula
**Companies**: All companies

### Pattern 16: Two Successive Changes — Find Original
**Concept**: Given final value after +a% then -b%, find original
**Difficulty**: Hard
**Approach**: Original = Final / [(1 + a/100)(1 - b/100)]
**Fastest**: Work backwards using reciprocal multipliers
**Companies**: Amazon, Goldman, Microsoft

### Pattern 17: Mixing Two Quantities at Different Percentages
**Concept**: Alligation at percentage level
**Difficulty**: Medium–Hard
**Approach**: Weighted average percentage = (sum of values × percentages) / total
**Fastest**: Weighted average formula
**Companies**: Deloitte, Goldman, Amazon

### Pattern 18: Time-Value Percentage Problems
**Concept**: Depreciation, appreciation over time
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: Use (1 ± r/100)^n, or for depreciation: (1 - r/100)^n
**Fastest**: Rule of 72 for doubling/halving times
**Companies**: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman

### Pattern 19: What Should the New Percentage Be?
**Concept**: "By what percent must sales increase to achieve target?"
**Difficulty**: Medium
**Approach**: (Target - Current) / Current × 100
**Fastest**: Direct formula
**Companies**: Infosys, Deloitte, Amazon

### Pattern 20: Percentage in Pie Chart DI
**Concept**: Extract percentage from visual, apply to total
**Difficulty**: Easy–Medium
**Approach**: Read off percentage, apply to total value
**Fastest**: Cross-multiply to verify percentages sum to 100

## ❌ Common Traps & Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Using New Value as Base for % Change
**Error**: Saying price increased 50% when it went from ₹200 to ₹300 (should use ₹200 as base)
**Fix**: Always identify the original value first. % change = (Change/Original) × 100.

### Mistake 2: Adding Percentages Directly for Successive Changes
**Error**: 20% increase then 20% decrease = 0% change (wrong!)
**Fix**: Use multipliers: 1.20 × 0.80 = 0.96 = 4% decrease.

### Mistake 3: Confusing Percentage Points with Percentage
**Error**: "Inflation fell 2% from 8%" → thinking it's 2% relative drop (it's actually 25% relative drop, or 2 percentage points)
**Fix**: When you see "percentage points," write down the raw difference. When you see "% change in rate," calculate relative change.

### Mistake 4: Multiplying Instead of Dividing in Reverse Percentage
**Error**: After 20% decrease, price is ₹400. Original = 400 × 0.80 = ₹320 (wrong!)
**Fix**: Original = 400 / 0.80 = ₹500. Divide to go backward.

### Mistake 5: Applying Discount on Cost Price Instead of Marked Price
**Error**: Cost ₹200, marked at 40% above CP, 20% discount. SP = 200 × 0.80 = ₹160 (wrong!)
**Fix**: MP = 200 × 1.40 = 280. SP = 280 × 0.80 = ₹224. Discount always applies on MP.

### Mistake 6: Forgetting That 0% and 100%+ Are Valid
**Error**: Thinking percentages can only range from 0% to 100%
**Fix**: 150% means 1.5 times original. 0% means zero. Both are valid outcomes.

### Mistake 7: Mixing Up "More Than" and "Less Than" Directions
**Error**: "A is 20% less than B" → writing A = B × 1.20 (should be 0.80)
**Fix**: "More than" → add (1 + r/100). "Less than" → subtract (1 - r/100).

### Mistake 8: Not Checking Answer in Context
**Error**: Getting 150% of 80 = 160, and saying it's 160% of 80 (correct) but then confusing the answer format
**Fix**: Always verify: 150% of 80 = 1.5 × 80 = 120. The 120 is the new value, 150 is the percentage.

### Mistake 9: Approximating Too Aggressively
**Error**: 33.3% ≈ 33% when the result should be exact
**Fix**: Know when exact is needed. In exam approximations, note the direction of error.

### Mistake 10: Chain Discount Calculation — Adding Instead of Formula
**Error**: Two discounts of 30% and 20% → total discount = 50% (wrong — actual is 44%)
**Fix**: Net discount = 30 + 20 - (30×20)/100 = 50 - 6 = 44%. Or: SP = MP × 0.70 × 0.80 = MP × 0.56 = 44% off.

   If bought at 10% less: 0.90x. Sold at 1.20x - 30.
   Profit = 40%: 1.20x - 30 = 0.90x × 1.40 = 1.26x
   1.20x - 30 = 1.26x → -30 = 0.06x → x = **₹500**
   </details>
12. In an election, 75% of voters voted. The winning candidate got 55% of valid votes and won by a margin of 4,200 votes. Find the total number of voters.
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Winner: 55% of valid votes, margin = 4200
   Winner - Runner-up: 55% - 45% = 10% of valid votes = 4200
   Valid votes = 42000
   Total voters = valid/0.75 = 42000/0.75 = **56,000**
   </details>
13. If the length of a rectangle increases by 30% and breadth decreases by 20%, what is the percentage change in area?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Length ↑ 30%, Breadth ↓ 20%
   New area = 1.30 × 0.80 = 1.04 → **4% increase**
   </details>
14. A man buys an article and sells it at a profit of 25%. If he had bought it at 20% less and sold it at ₹40 more, his profit would be 65%. Find the cost price.
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Let CP = x, SP = 1.25x
   If bought at 20% less: 0.80x. Sold at 1.25x + 40.
   Profit = 65%: 1.25x + 40 = 0.80x × 1.65 = 1.32x
   1.25x + 40 = 1.32x → 40 = 0.07x → x = **₹571.43**
   </details>
15. The population of a town is 20,000. It increases at 10% for the first year and 20% for the second year. In the third year, it decreases by 10%. What is the population at the end of 3 years?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   20000 → +10% → +20% → -10%
   20000 × 1.10 × 1.20 × 0.90
   = 20000 × 1.10 × 1.08
   = 20000 × 1.188 = **23,760**
   </details>
16. Two discounts of x% each are equivalent to a single discount of 36%. Find x.
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Two discounts of x% each → net = 36%
   x + x - x²/100 = 36
   2x - x²/100 = 36
   200x - x² = 3600
   x² - 200x + 3600 = 0
   (x-20)(x-180) = 0 → x = 20 (reject x=180, discount can't be 180%)
   **x = 20%**
   </details>
17. The price of a shirt is first increased by 10%, then decreased by 10%. The shopkeeper then offers a buy-one-get-one-free offer. What is the effective percentage discount?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   +10%, -10%, then buy-1-get-1-free (BOGO)
   After +10%, -10%: back to original (1.10 × 0.90 = 0.99 ≈ 99%)
   BOGO: Pay for 1, get 2 → effectively 50% of price.
   Effective discount = **50%** (pays 50% of original)
   </details>
18. In a school, 60% are boys. 20% of boys and 10% of girls are below 10 years of age. What percentage of students below 10 are girls?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Boys below 10: 20% of 60% = 12% of total
   Girls below 10: 10% of 40% = 4% of total
   Total below 10 = 12% + 4% = 16% of total
   Girls among below-10 = 4% / 16% × 100 = **25%**
   </details>
19. A shopkeeper sells goods at cost price but uses a false weight of 900g instead of 1kg. What is his profit percentage?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   False weight: 900g = 1kg (gives 900g when buyer thinks 1000g)
   Profit = (1000-900)/900 × 100 = 100/900 × 100 = **11.11%**
   </details>
20. The price of a commodity increases by 10% in 2020, 20% in 2021, and decreases by 10% in 2022. If the initial price was ₹1,000, find the final price.
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   1000 → +10% → +20% → -10%
   1000 × 1.10 × 1.20 × 0.90
   = 1000 × 1.10 × 1.08
   = 1000 × 1.188 = **₹1,188**

   ---
   </details>

### 🏆 Product-Based Company Level Questions (1-10)

1. The price of a share was ₹100 on Jan 1. It increased by 20% each month for 3 months, then decreased by 15% each month for 2 months. What is the final price?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   +20% per month for 3 months, then -15% per month for 2 months
   100 × (1.20)³ × (0.85)²
   = 100 × 1.728 × 0.7225
   = 100 × 1.24848
   = **₹124.85**
   </details>
2. A company has 1,000 employees. 60% are men. 30% of men and 20% of women have engineering degrees. If 10 engineers are to be selected for a project, what is the probability (as a percentage) that a randomly selected engineer is a woman?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Total employees: 1000. Men: 600, Women: 400.
   Men with engineering: 30% of 600 = 180
   Women with engineering: 20% of 400 = 80
   Total engineers = 260. Women engineers = 80.
   Probability woman engineer = 80/260 × 100 = **30.77%**
   </details>
3. The population of a village was 10,000 in 2010. It grew at 8% per year. In what year will it cross 25,000?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Population 10000, 8% growth, cross 25000
   10000 × (1.08)^n = 25000
   (1.08)^n = 2.5
   n × log(1.08) = log(2.5)
   n = log(2.5)/log(1.08) = 0.3979/0.0334 ≈ 11.9 years
   From 2010: **2022** (crosses in late 2022)
   </details>
4. An item is sold at a profit of 20%. If the cost price is reduced by 15% and selling price remains the same, what is the new profit percentage?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Original: CP = x, SP = 1.20x
   New CP = 0.85x, SP unchanged = 1.20x
   New profit = (1.20x - 0.85x) / 0.85x × 100 = 0.35x/0.85x × 100 = **41.18%**
   </details>
5. A tank is 80% full. After removing 40 litres, it becomes 60% full. The tank's full capacity is what percent more than the current water amount?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Full capacity = C.
   80% full → 0.80C. Remove 40L → 60% full → 0.60C.
   40L = 0.20C → C = 200L
   Current water = 0.80C = 160L
   Full capacity is 200L, current is 160L.
   % more = (200-160)/160 × 100 = 40/160 × 100 = **25% more**
   </details>
6. A man buys goods worth ₹10,000. He sells half at a profit of 20% and the rest at a loss of 10%. At what percent profit or loss must he sell the remaining goods to achieve an overall profit of 10%?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Total cost = 10000. Half at 20% profit, half at 10% loss.
   Half goods: cost = 5000, SP = 5000 × 1.20 = 6000
   Other half: cost = 5000, SP = 5000 × 0.90 = 4500
   Total SP so far = 10500
   Need 10% overall profit on 10000: Total SP needed = 11000
   Remaining goods cost = 5000. Need SP = 11000 - 10500 = 500.
   % profit on remaining = (500-5000)/5000 × 100 = -80%
   He needs to sell at a **80% loss** on the remaining goods — which means he needs to reconsider his strategy or the problem implies he can sell remaining at a higher price.
   </details>
7. The side of a square is increased by 5% and then decreased by 5%. The area of the resulting figure is what percent less than the original area?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Side +5% then -5%
   New side = 1.05 × 0.95 × s = 0.9975s ≈ 99.75% of original
   Area = (0.9975)² = 0.9950 → **0.5% decrease**
   </details>
8. In an examination, 60% passed in Mathematics, 50% passed in Physics, and 40% passed in both. 500 students failed in both subjects. Find the total number of students.
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Passed in Math = 60%, Passed in Physics = 50%, Both = 40%
   Passed in at least one: 60+50-40 = 70%
   Failed in both = 30% = 500 students
   Total students = 500/0.30 = **1666.67** ≈ 1667 (round to whole)
   </details>
9. A sum of money becomes 3 times itself in 5 years at simple interest. What is the rate of interest? What would it be at compound interest?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Simple Interest: sum becomes 3× in 5 years
   SI = 3P - P = 2P. Rate = (2P × 100)/(P × 5) = **40% per annum** (very high)
   Compound Interest: Find rate where (1+r)^5 = 3
   r = 3^(1/5) - 1 = 1.2457 - 1 = 0.2457 = **24.57% per annum**
   </details>
10. The price of a commodity falls by 20% each year for 3 consecutive years. By what percent must it increase in the fourth year to return to the original price?
   <details>
   <summary>🔍 View Solution</summary>

   Falls 20% per year for 3 years, need 4th year increase to return to original.
   After 3 years: (0.80)³ = 0.512 (51.2% remains)
   Need to go from 51.2% back to 100%.
   Increase needed = (100-51.2)/51.2 × 100 = 48.8/51.2 × 100 = **95.31%**
   Or: just compute 1/0.512 = 1.9531 → increase of **95.31%**

   ---
   </details>

### The One Thing to Remember

**x% of y = y% of x** — this single identity, internalized, will save you 5-10 seconds per question. Before calculating 8% of 25, pause for half a second and ask: "Can I swap this to 25% of 8?"

That mindset — always looking for the easier path — is what separates top scorers from average ones in percentage questions.
