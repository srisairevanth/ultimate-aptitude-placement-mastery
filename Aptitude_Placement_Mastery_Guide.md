## Executive Summary

This guide identifies the **15 aptitude topics** that appear most frequently and carry the highest weightage in placement tests at service giants (TCS, Infosys, Accenture, Cognizant, Capgemini, Wipro, Deloitte) and product companies (Amazon, Microsoft, Google, Flipkart, Paytm). Each topic is ranked by ROI — meaning how likely you are to face it and how much it contributes to your score.

**Assumptions:** You study 1.5 hours/day on weekdays and 2.5 hours on weekends. No prior aptitude background is assumed — this is a structured sprint from zero to placement-ready.

## The Top 15 Topics — Ranked by Priority

### 🥇 Rank 1: Number Systems & Simplifications

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> Number Systems is the single most foundational topic in quantitative aptitude. It appears in virtually every placement test — often 5–8 questions in first-round online assessments. It also underlies divisibility, HCF/LCM, remainder theorems, and surds — all of which are standalone topics themselves. Master this and three other topics become trivially easier.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Place value and face value
- Divisibility rules (2 through 13)
- HCF and LCM via prime factorisation and Euclidean algorithm
- Remainder theorem: when N is divided by a divisor, remainder r means N ≡ r (mod divisor)
- Consecutive integer properties: sum = n(n+1)/2, product of first n odds = n!
- Cyclicity of last digits (2, 3, 7, 8 cycle every 4)
- Finding the last two digits of large powers (use modulo 100)
- Square root and cube root shortcuts
- Simplification using BODMAS and digit sum tricks

> [!TIP]
> **Common Test/Interview Patterns**
> 1. "Find the remainder when 7^42 is divided by 8" (use Fermat's little theorem or cyclicity)
2. "If the HCF of two numbers is 12 and their LCM is 360, find the numbers" (a×b = HCF×LCM)
3. "A number leaves remainder 3 when divided by 5 and remainder 5 when divided by 7. Find the smallest such number" (Chinese Remainder Theorem — CRT)
4. "Find the unit digit of (123)^45 + (456)^78"
5. "What is the largest 4-digit number divisible by 3, 5, and 8?"

**Difficulty:** Medium (Easy if you build strong fundamentals; Hard when remainder theorems get tricky)

**Practice questions for mastery:** 75–100 questions

### 🥈 Rank 2: Percentages

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> Percentages are the connective tissue of quant. They appear directly in percentage-based questions AND as the underlying mechanism for Profit & Loss, Simple/Compound Interest, Discounts, Data Interpretation, and Successive Percentage changes. You cannot afford to be weak here. Nearly every placement test has 3–5 direct percentage questions and 5–8 indirect ones.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Conversion: X% = X/100. Fractions to percentages: 1/6 ≈ 16.67%, 1/7 ≈ 14.28%, 1/8 = 12.5%, 1/9 ≈ 11.11%, 1/12 ≈ 8.33%
- Percentage increase/decrease: New = Original × (1 ± p/100)
- Successive percentage change: Final = Original × (1+p1/100) × (1+p2/100) × ...
- Expressing one quantity as % of another: (A/B) × 100
- Percentage vs. absolute change: know when each matters
- Population and depreciation formulas (same structure as compound interest)
- Base concept: when percentage is applied to a changing base, the reference changes

**Common patterns:**
1. "If the price of a commodity increases by 20% and then decreases by 20%, what is the net change?" (trick: result is NOT zero — always compute)
2. "A man spends 75% of his income. If his income increases by 22% and expenditure by 10%, by how much does his savings increase?"
3. "Two successive discounts of 20% and 30% are equivalent to a single discount of what percent?"
4. "In an election, 75% of voters voted. 2% of the voted ballots were invalid. If candidate A got 40% of valid votes and won by 3,420 votes, how many voters were registered?"

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### 🥉 Rank 3: Profit, Loss & Discount

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> Every placement test includes at least 2–4 profit/loss questions. They test commercial awareness and numerical reasoning simultaneously. Marked price, discount, successive discounts, and break-even problems are particularly common in service company tests. This topic is a direct extension of percentages.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Cost Price (CP), Selling Price (SP), Marked Price (MP), Discount %
- Profit% = (SP–CP)/CP × 100; Loss% = (CP–SP)/CP × 100
- Discount is always on Marked Price, not CP
- SP = MP × (1 – discount/100)
- When discount% and profit% are given on the same transaction, relate CP and MP
- False weights: if a merchant uses a weight less than 1 kg, his gain% = ((True weight – False weight)/False weight) × 100
- Selling at cost price but giving less quantity (adulteration/false weight problem)
- Equivalent single discount: (1–d1/100)(1–d2/100)(1–d3/100) = (1–D_eq/100)

**Common patterns:**
1. "A shopkeeper gives a discount of 20% on MP but still makes a 10% profit. If CP is Rs. 900, find MP."
2. "A merchant sells rice at a profit of 20%. If he reduces the price by 10%, his profit becomes?"
3. "Two successive discounts of 15% and 25% are offered. What single discount is equivalent?"
4. "A shopkeeper uses a false weight of 900g for 1kg. At what price per kg should he sell to make a 20% profit?" (Use gain% formula above)
5. "The cost price of 20 articles equals the selling price of 15 articles. Find profit%." (SP_20 = CP_20; SP_15/CP_15...)

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### Rank 4: Simple & Compound Interest

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> SI and CI are perennially tested, especially in bank and IT service company tests. Compound Interest questions with the concept of "interest on interest" and population/growth depreciation problems are favorite interview talking points. The formulas are limited — once you understand the mechanism, this topic becomes a high-scoring gift.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - SI = P × R × T / 100; Amount = P(1 + RT/100)
- CI: A = P(1 + R/100)^T ; CI = A – P
- Population/growth: P(1 + r/100)^n
- Depreciation: P(1 – r/100)^n
- When rate is compounded half-yearly: R/2, T×2; quarterly: R/4, T×4
- Difference between CI and SI for 2 years: D = P(R/100)^2/100
- Simple interest remains constant; compound interest grows each period
- Installments: EMI problems using present value concept (often asked in loans)

**Common patterns:**
1. "A sum of Rs. 5000 becomes Rs. 6050 in 2 years at CI. Find the rate."
2. "The CI on a sum at 10% per annum for 2 years is Rs. 210. Find the SI on the same sum for the same period." (Use D = P(R/100)^2/100 → P = 21000)
3. "A sum at CI doubles itself in 5 years. In how many years will it become 8 times?"
4. "The population of a town is 100,000. It increases at 5% per annum. What will it be after 3 years?"
5. "A man borrowed Rs. 20,000 at 12% p.a. SI and repaid in two equal annual installments. Find installment amount." (Present value = sum of PVs of installments)

**Difficulty:** Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 50–70 questions

### Rank 5: Time & Work

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> Time and Work problems are a staple of placement tests. They test proportional reasoning and efficiency concepts. Questions on combined work, alternate days, and work-leave problems are particularly tricky and make excellent interview discussion topics. The underlying concept (work = rate × time) is also used in Pipes & Cisterns.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - If A can complete a work in x days → A's 1-day work = 1/x
- Work = Rate × Time; Total work = 1 unit
- Combined work: 1/A + 1/B = 1/Combined_time
- If three workers A, B, C work together: 1/A + 1/B + 1/C = 1/T_combined
- If A is twice as efficient as B → A's rate = 2 × B's rate → A takes half B's time
- Work leaving problems: "A works for 3 days, then B joins. Work finished in X more days. Find B's time."
- Man-days formula: Work = Number of men × Days × Efficiency
- Chain rule: more men → less time (inversely proportional, for same efficiency)
- Pipelines (Pipes & Cisterns variant): fill rate, drain rate, net rate

**Common patterns:**
1. "A can do a work in 20 days, B in 30 days. They work together for 5 days, then A leaves. How long does B take to finish?"
2. "A is twice as efficient as B. Together they complete a work in 12 days. How long would A alone take?"
3. "If 10 men can do a work in 6 days, how many days will 15 men take?" (assuming equal efficiency)
4. "A and B work on alternate days. A starts. They finish in X days. Find B's time alone." (Use half-day work rates)
5. "A pipe fills a tank in 10 hours but due to a leak it takes 15 hours. How long does the leak take to empty the full tank?" (net rate = fill rate – leak rate)

**Difficulty:** Medium (Hard when multiple workers and alternating days are combined)

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### Rank 6: Time, Speed & Distance

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★★)**
> TSD questions are the most practical topic in aptitude — they map to real-world scenarios that interviewers love to discuss. Concepts like relative speed, trains crossing each other, upstream/downstream, and average speed generate elegant, solvable problems. They also serve as the foundation for Boats & Streams.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Speed = Distance / Time
- Units: convert km/hr ↔ m/sec: multiply/divide by 3.6
- Average speed (weighted): Total distance / Total time (NOT simple average of speeds)
- Relative speed: approaching = sum of speeds; same direction = difference
- Train problems: Length of train + Length of platform/object; time = total length / relative speed
- Circular tracks: same direction → LCM-based meeting time; opposite direction → faster approach
- Upstream: speed = (boat speed – river speed); Downstream: speed = (boat speed + river speed)
- Boat in still water: (Up + Down)/2; River speed: (Down – Up)/2
- Early/late problems: same distance, different times → find distance or speed
- Rendezvous problems: two persons starting from A and B → meet at time = distance / relative speed

**Common patterns:**
1. "A train 200m long passes a man walking at 5 km/h in 36 seconds. Find the train's speed."
2. "Two trains 150m and 200m long run towards each other at 40 km/h and 50 km/h. How long to cross completely?"
3. "A man goes to a place at 30 km/h and returns at 20 km/h. His average speed for the whole journey?"
4. "A boat goes 30 km upstream and 44 km downstream in 10 hours. Its speed in still water is 10 km/h. Find the speed of the stream."
5. "A person covers a certain distance at 3 km/h and returns at 6 km/h. Total time = 3 hours. Find total distance."

**Difficulty:** Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### Rank 7: Averages, Ratio & Proportion

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★☆)**
> Averages and ratios appear in nearly every placement test, often 2–4 questions each. Ratio problems frequently combine with mixture, age, and partnership questions. The average formula is deceptively simple but the concept of weighted averages is where most candidates fail — this is exactly where you can distinguish yourself.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Average = Sum of values / Number of values
- Weighted average: Avg = (n1×a1 + n2×a2) / (n1 + n2)
- When adding or removing a value, use deviation from original average
- If average of n numbers is A and one number x is replaced by y: new average = A + (y–x)/n
- Ratio: a/b; if a:b = 3:5 and a+10 = b → solve simultaneously
- Proportion: a:b = c:d → ad = bc (cross-multiply)
- Dividing a quantity in a ratio: share = (ratio part / sum of ratios) × total quantity
- Compound ratio: (a:b) × (c:d) = (ac : bd)
- Direct proportion: when one increases, the other increases proportionally (y = kx)
- Indirect/inverse proportion: when one increases, the other decreases (y = k/x)

**Common patterns:**
1. "The average of 15 numbers is 25. If one number is removed, the average becomes 23. What was the removed number?" (use: removed = old total – new total)
2. "The average weight of 8 persons increases by 2 kg when a new person replaces one weighing 60 kg. Find the weight of the new person." (new total = old total + diff)
3. "A mixture contains milk and water in ratio 5:3. When 10 liters of water is added, the ratio becomes 5:4. Find original quantities." (set up equation)
4. "Divide Rs. 1170 among A, B, C in ratio 1/2 : 2/3 : 3/4." (first convert to like fractions)
5. "The average of 10 numbers is 12.5. Later it is found one number 18 was misread as 28. Find correct average."

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 50–70 questions

### Rank 8: Mixtures & Alligations

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★☆)**
> Mixture problems are a direct application of weighted average concepts. The Alligation method is one of the most elegant shortcuts in aptitude — it solves mixture problems in seconds that would otherwise require algebra. This technique is also used in Profit & Loss (finding average cost) and in some Data Interpretation calculations. Questions on replacing a part of a mixture are particularly common.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Alligation rule: (Cost of cheaper – Mean) : (Mean – Cost of dearer) = Quantity of dearer : Quantity of cheaper
- When two ingredients are mixed: mean price = (q1×p1 + q2×p2) / (q1+q2)
- Replacement method: After n replacements, quantity remaining = Initial × (1 – r/total)^n (where r = replaced amount, total = total mixture)
- Alligation diagram for three or more ingredients
- Mixing two varieties to get a third price
- Difference between mixture and alligation: mixture is the process, alligation is the visual technique
- Transpose method: same formula as alligation but derived differently

**Common patterns:**
1. "In what ratio must rice at Rs. 20/kg and Rs. 30/kg be mixed to get a mixture worth Rs. 24/kg?" (Use alligation: 30–24 : 24–20 = 6:4 = 3:2)
2. "A container has 60 liters of milk. 12 liters are removed and replaced with water. Again 12 liters of mixture is removed and replaced. How much milk remains?" (use replacement formula twice)
3. "The average age of 10 students is 20 years. When a teacher joins, the average becomes 22 years. Find the teacher's age."
4. "Two alloys contain gold and silver in ratios 3:5 and 5:7. In what ratio should they be mixed to get an alloy with gold:silver = 5:7?"
5. "A dishonest shopkeeper sells at cost price but uses a false weight of 850g for 1kg. Find his gain%."

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 50–70 questions

### Rank 9: Probability

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★☆)**
> Probability is a firm favorite in product company interviews (Amazon, Microsoft, Google). These companies value probabilistic reasoning in system design and data analysis. Questions range from basic dice/coin/card problems to conditional probability and Bayes' theorem applications. The topic is also conceptually rich enough to generate excellent interview discussions.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - P(E) = Favorable outcomes / Total outcomes
- Sample space: all possible outcomes
- Mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B)
- Independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
- Complementary events: P(E) + P(E') = 1
- Conditional probability: P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B)
- Bayes' Theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A) × P(A) / P(B) [higher-level, asked at product companies]
- Expected value: E(X) = Σ x × P(x)
- Permutations and combinations as the foundation for counting favorable outcomes

**Common patterns:**
1. "Two dice are thrown. What is the probability that the sum is 8?" (count pairs: 5 pairs → 5/36)
2. "A bag has 5 red and 4 white balls. Two balls are drawn one after another without replacement. Find P(both red)."
3. "A speaks truth 3/4 of the time and B speaks truth 2/3 of the time. They both report that a coin toss showed heads. Find P it actually was heads."
4. "From a deck of 52 cards, two cards are drawn. Find P(at least one is a king)."
5. "Three persons A, B, C have probabilities 1/3, 1/4, 1/5 of solving a problem respectively. Find P(the problem is solved)."

**Difficulty:** Medium to Hard

**Practice questions for mastery:** 50–80 questions

### Rank 10: Permutations & Combinations

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★☆)**
> P&C is the backbone of probability and appears as a standalone topic in most placement tests. It is conceptually dense — the biggest mistake candidates make is confusing permutations (order matters) with combinations (order doesn't matter). Product company interviews frequently ask P&C-based brain teasers to test structured thinking.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Fundamental principle of counting: multiply for "and" (A and B both happen), add for "or" (either A or B)
- nPr = n! / (n–r)! — arrangements where order matters
- nCr = n! / r!(n–r)! — selections where order does NOT matter
- Key distinction: permutation = arrangement = order matters; combination = selection = order doesn't matter
- Circular permutations: (n–1)! for distinct arrangements around a circle
- Permutations with repetition: n! / (r1! × r2! × ...) when some items are identical
- Complement method: "at least one" = 1 – P(none)
- "At least one" problems often easier via complement: P(at least one success) = 1 – (P(no success))^n

**Common patterns:**
1. "How many 4-digit numbers can be formed using digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 without repetition?"
2. "In how many ways can 3 boys and 3 girls be arranged in a row so that no two boys are together?" (Use gap method)
3. "How many different words can be formed from the letters of 'EQUATION' such that vowels always come together?" (Treat vowel group as one unit)
4. "A committee of 5 is to be formed from 6 men and 5 women. In how many ways if it must have at least 2 women?"
5. "How many 3-digit numbers are divisible by 5?" (Use arithmetic progression or counting method)

**Difficulty:** Medium to Hard

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### Rank 11: Ages

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★☆☆)**
> Ages problems are deceptively simple — they look like basic algebra but trip up candidates who don't set up the equations correctly. They test the ability to handle variable relationships across time. These problems appear in 1–3 questions per test and are high-scoring if you know the common patterns. They also sharpen algebra skills useful in other topics.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Present ages: if A's age = x, B's age = y
- Age n years ago: (x – n), (y – n)
- Age n years hence: (x + n), (y + n)
- Ratio of ages is preserved when both are multiplied by same factor
- Sum of ages of a group remains constant (when people are not added or removed)
- If ages are in ratio a:b and given sum → individual ages
- "n years ago" problems: set up equation with ages at that time
- Average age problems: connect to Averages topic
- Age puzzles with multiple people and conditions → system of equations

**Common patterns:**
1. "The ratio of ages of A and B is 4:3. A is 8 years older than B. Find their ages." (simple: 4x – 3x = 8 → x = 8)
2. "A father is 4 times as old as his son. In 8 years, he will be 2.5 times as old. Find their current ages." (set up: 4x+8 = 2.5(x+8))
3. "Five years ago, the sum of ages of a father and son was 60. Find the father's current age if the son will be half the father's age in 5 years."
4. "The average age of 20 students is 15 years. When the teacher joins, the average becomes 16 years. Find the teacher's age."
5. "A is twice as old as B was 5 years ago. B is twice as old as C is now. If the sum of their ages is 35, find A's age."

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 40–60 questions

### Rank 12: Coding & Decoding (Alphabet/Number Series)

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★☆☆)**
> Coding-Decoding is the most reliable topic in the Logical Reasoning section of placement tests. These questions are formulaic — once you learn the common code patterns, you can solve them consistently in under 30 seconds each. They appear in nearly every online assessment. This topic also sharpens your analytical pattern-recognition ability, which is useful for data interpretation.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Letter position: A=1, B=2 ... Z=26; reverse: Z=1, Y=2 ... A=26
- Position arithmetic: if each letter is shifted by n positions → Caesar cipher
- Opposite letter: A↔Z, B↔Y (numerical sum = 27)
- Vowel/consonant codes: apply different rules to each group
- Word-based coding: use first/last letter, number of letters, or alphabetical positions
- Matrix-based coding: two sets of letters in rows/columns
- Pattern in number-letter mixed series: positional logic, alternating series
- Coding with conditions: "if in a certain code, A is written as Z and B as Y, how is CAT written?"

**Common patterns:**
1. "In a certain code, FROZEN is coded as MGHUDS. How is FLOWER coded?" (shift each letter forward by 3)
2. "In a certain language, 'TRAIN' is called '12345', 'RAIN' is '2345'. What is the code for 'TRAI'?" (position-based)
3. "If PAINT is coded as 74152 and EXCEL is coded as 93578, how is TAPE coded?" (map letters to numbers, then find number for T)
4. "In a row of letters, find the odd one out or the next letter: A, C, F, J, O, ..."
5. "In a certain code, 'MASTER' is written as 'TAMRES'. How is 'CLUSTER' written?" (reverse pairs or specific pattern)

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 60–80 questions

### Rank 13: Blood Relations

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★☆☆)**
> Blood relation puzzles are consistently asked in logical reasoning sections. They test your ability to decode family tree relationships from coded descriptions — a skill that translates to structured problem-solving in interviews. These problems often combine with sitting arrangement or puzzle-based questions.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Relations: father, mother, brother, sister, son, daughter, grandfather, grandmother, uncle, aunt, nephew, niece, cousin
- Key distinction: maternal vs. paternal; elder vs. younger
- Generation counting: "My son's son" = grandson; "my father's only son" = brother (or self)
- Family tree method: draw from the speaker's perspective
- Coded blood relations: A+B means A is B's sister; solve the tree
- "A is B's sister. B is C's mother. D is C's brother. How is D related to A?" (trace: C→B→A: D is C's brother; A is B's sister; B is C's mother → D and A are siblings/cousins → A and D are siblings)
- Tautology tricks: "A's only sibling's mother" = A's mother

**Common patterns:**
1. "Pointing to a man, a woman said, 'His mother is the only daughter of my mother.' How is the woman related to the man?" (Only daughter of my mother = myself → she is his mother)
2. "A is B's sister. C is B's mother. D is C's father. E is D's mother. How is B related to D?" (B is C's child; C is D's child → B is D's grandchild)
3. "A is B's son. C is B's mother. D is C's father. E is D's mother. How is A related to D?" (B is A's father; C is B's mother; D is C's father → A is D's great-grandson)
4. "In a family of 6 persons, A is the father of B. C is the mother of A. D is the brother of A. E is the sister of C. Who is B's uncle?" (B's uncle = A's brother = D)
5. "Introducing a man, a woman said, 'He is the son of my mother's husband.' How is the man related to the woman?" (my mother's husband = my father → his son = my brother)

**Difficulty:** Easy to Medium

**Practice questions for mastery:** 50–70 questions

### Rank 14: Clocks & Calendar

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★☆☆)**
> Clock and Calendar problems have a reputation for being intimidating, but they're entirely formula-driven — once you know the standard formulas, you can solve any question in under a minute. They appear 1–2 times per test and are guaranteed high-scoring if you've prepared them. Calendar problems also require understanding of leap years and odd days.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> **Clocks:**
- Minute hand moves 360° in 60 min → 6° per minute
- Hour hand moves 360° in 12 hours → 0.5° per minute = 30° per hour
- Relative speed = 5.5° per minute (hour hand relative to minute hand)
- Angle between hands = |30H – 5.5M| where H = hour, M = minutes past hour
- If hands are together: angle = 0 → M = 60H/11 minutes past H
- Mirror/distance problems: reflection time = 11 hours – given time (for times between 12 and 11)

**Calendar:**
- Odd days = (total days – nearest multiple of 7)
- Ordinary year: 365 days → 52 weeks + 1 odd day
- Leap year: 366 days → 52 weeks + 2 odd days
- Leap year: divisible by 4, EXCEPT centuries (unless divisible by 400)
- Days from reference: Jan 1, 1 AD was Monday (for calculations)
- Finding the day of the week for any given date using Zeller's congruence or odd day counting

**Common patterns:**
1. "Find the angle between the hands of a clock at 3:40." (|30×3 – 5.5×40| = |90 – 220| = 130°)
2. "At what time between 3 and 4 o'clock are the hands of a clock in a straight line?" (angle = 180° → |90 – 5.5M| = 180)
3. "The minute hand of a clock overtakes the hour hand every 65 minutes. How much time does it gain in a day?" (actual gain = 65 vs. normal 60 → gains 5/11 min/day per overtaking)
4. "What day of the week was 15th August 1947?" (use odd day calculation: anchor from known day)
5. "If today is 1st January 2024 (Monday), what day will it be on 1st January 2025?" (ordinary year = +1 odd day → Tuesday)

**Difficulty:** Medium (easy with formulas, hard without)

**Practice questions for mastery:** 40–60 questions

### Rank 15: Data Interpretation

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Why It Matters (★★★★☆)**
> Data Interpretation is the final mile of quantitative aptitude. In placement tests, the DI section is often make-or-break — it typically carries 5–10 questions in a single caselet (bar chart, pie chart, table, line graph, or mixed). The key skill here is speed: extracting numbers quickly and performing calculations accurately. This topic also appears in descriptive interview rounds where candidates explain their reasoning.

> [!NOTE]
> **Key Concepts to Master**
> - Reading bar graphs, pie charts, line graphs, tables, and mixed charts
- Percentage calculations from pie charts: sector angle = (component/total) × 360°
- Bar graph comparisons: read values, compute differences and percentages
- Line graph trends: growth/decline, identify peak and trough years
- Table-based DI: focus on relevant rows/columns, ignore extraneous data
- Mixed DI: combine data from multiple chart types in one question
- Approximation techniques: round numbers to make calculations faster
- Finding averages and percentages from charts directly (don't compute from individual values)
- DI caselet: read the paragraph, extract given data, solve using formulas from earlier topics

**Common patterns:**
1. Pie chart: "If the total budget is Rs. 50 lakhs, and the Education sector is 18% of the pie, what is the education budget?"

## Quick Reference: Key Formulas at a Glance

**Number Systems:**
- n! contains trailing zeros = ⌊n/5⌋ + ⌊n/25⌋ + ⌊n/125⌋ + ...
- (a^n − b^n) is divisible by (a − b)

**Percentages:**
- Successive change: Final = Original × (1 ± p1/100) × (1 ± p2/100) × ...

**Profit & Loss:**
- Equivalent discount = 1 − (1−d1/100)(1−d2/100)
- Gain% using false weight = (True − False)/False × 100

**SI & CI:**
- CI − SI for 2 years = P × (R/100)²
- Population/growth: A = P(1 + r/100)^n

**Time & Work:**
- Combined: 1/A + 1/B = 1/T
- Man × Days × Hours = Work (constant)

**TSD:**
- Average speed (different speeds over same distance) = 2ab/(a+b)
- Train crossing: time = (L1 + L2)/relative speed

**Probability:**
- P(at least one) = 1 − P(none)
- P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)

**P&C:**
- nPr = n!/(n−r)!
- nCr = n!/(r!(n−r)!)
- Circular: (n−1)! arrangements

## 5 Habits of Top 5% Scorers

1. **Timed practice sessions:** Never solve without a timer. Target 60 seconds per question.
2. **Concept before shortcuts:** Understand WHY a formula works. Then learn the shortcut. This prevents formula confusion under pressure.
3. **Daily revision:** Spend 20 minutes each morning revising formulas from the previous day's topic. Retention is 80% of the battle.
4. **Mock tests from Day 1:** Take one sectional test every 3 days starting from Day 4. This builds test stamina and identifies weak areas early.
5. **Error log:** Maintain a notebook of mistakes. Review it the night before the exam. Most repeated errors are avoidable.
