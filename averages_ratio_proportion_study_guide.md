## 📖 Concept Overview

### What Are Averages, Ratio & Proportion?

**Averages** express a single representative value for a set of numbers — the central tendency.

**Ratio** expresses the relative size of two quantities — how many times one is of the other.

**Proportion** expresses that two ratios are equal — a relationship of equivalence.

Together, these three concepts form the framework through which we compare, scale, and reason about quantities in every quantitative domain.

### 1. Simple Average (Arithmetic Mean)

**Definition**: The sum of quantities divided by the number of quantities.

**Formula**:
```
Average = (Sum of all items) / (Number of items)
Sum = Average × Number of items
```

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Think of it as the "equal share" — if everyone got the same value, what would it need to be for the total to be unchanged?

**Example**: Marks in 5 subjects: 70, 80, 65, 90, 75.
Sum = 380, Count = 5.
Average = 380/5 = **76**

**The Deviation Method**: Instead of adding all numbers, calculate average of known values, then adjust:
- Known average: 50 for 10 items → total = 500
- New item: 65 → new average = (500+65)/11 = 565/11 = 51.36

> [!TIP]
> **Shortcut Method**
> For numbers close to a reference value, find average of deviations from that reference.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Confusing weighted average with simple average. When groups have different sizes, the combined average is not the arithmetic mean of the group averages.

---

### 2. Weighted Average

**Definition**: When combining groups of different sizes, weight each group's average by its size.

**Formula**:
```
Weighted Average = (Sum₁ + Sum₂ + ...) / (n₁ + n₂ + ...)
= (n₁A₁ + n₂A₂) / (n₁ + n₂)
```

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Think of it as a class with different section sizes. A class of 30 averaging 80 and a class of 70 averaging 60 doesn't average to 70 — the larger class pulls the average down.

**Example**: Class A: 30 students, average 80. Class B: 70 students, average 60.
Combined average = (30×80 + 70×60) / 100 = (2400 + 4200) / 100 = **66**

**The Balance Method**: Think of the deviation from a candidate average. The total positive deviation equals total negative deviation at the true average.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Taking the arithmetic mean of group averages: (80+60)/2 = 70. The correct answer is 66 because the 70-student class (at 60) is larger.

---

### 3. Adding and Removing from a Group

**Concept**: When one or more members enter or leave a group, the average changes. Use the change to find the unknown.

**Adding a Member**:
```
New Average = A_new
Let added value = x
(n × A_old + x) / (n+1) = A_new
x = (n+1)A_new - n × A_old
```

**Removing a Member**:
```
(n × A_old - x) / (n-1) = A_new
x = n × A_old - (n-1) × A_new
```

**Adding Multiple Members** at once:
```
Total added = sum of new members
n_new × A_new + n × A_old = (n + n_new) × A_combined
```

**Example**: Average of 15 numbers is 24. A new number increases the average to 26. What is the new number?
15×24 = 360. 16×26 = 416.
New number = 416 - 360 = **56**

**Example**: Average of 20 numbers is 40. One number is replaced by another, and the average remains 40. What is the replacement number?
Old total = 800. New total = 800 (average unchanged).
Replacement = same value → **any number equal to the replaced number.**

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students forget to account for the change in group size. When adding one person, the denominator increases by 1.

---

### 4. Replacement Problems (Swapping Members)

**Concept**: When one member of a group is replaced, find the replacing member's value.

**Formula**:
```
Replaced value = n × A_old - (n × A_new)
```

Wait — actually, when a member is replaced:
- Old total = n × A_old
- New total = n × A_new
- Change in total = n(A_new - A_old) = New member - Old member
- New member = Old member + n(A_new - A_old)

**Example**: Average of 20 students is 50 kg. One student weighing 45 kg is replaced. The new average is 51 kg. Find the new student's weight.
Total change = 20(51 - 50) = 20 kg.
New weight = 45 + 20 = **65 kg**

**Variant — Finding the replaced value**:
Old member = New member - n(A_new - A_old)

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Adding the difference instead of subtracting, or forgetting the direction of change.

### 5. Alligation — Two Quantities

**Concept**: When mixing two ingredients at different prices/costs to get a desired average price.

**The Alligation Method**:
```
Write the cheaper price on the left, dearer on the right.
Write the mean price (desired average) in the middle.
Cross-subtract to find the ratio.
```

**Visual**:
```
Cheaper: a ---- mean: m ---- Dearer: b
Ratio of cheaper : dearer = (b - m) : (m - a)
```

**Example**: Mix rice at ₹30/kg and ₹45/kg to get ₹36/kg.
- a = 30, b = 45, m = 36
- Ratio = (45-36) : (36-30) = 9 : 6 = **3 : 2**
- To get 5 kg of mixture: cheaper = 5 × 3/5 = **3 kg**, dearer = **2 kg**

**Shortcut — The Formula**:
```
Quantity of cheaper = (Mean price - Dearer price) / (Cheaper - Dearer)  [in absolute terms]
Quantity ratio = (Desired mean - Dearer) : (Cheaper - Desired mean)
```

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> The deviation of each price from the mean determines how much of the other is needed to balance it.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Getting the ratio reversed. Cheaper ingredient gets the difference from the dearer side.

---

### 6. Alligation — Multiple Quantities

**Concept**: Mixing more than two ingredients.

**Method**: Use the alligation tree or successive alligation.

**Successive Alligation**:
1. Mix two at a time using the alligation rule
2. The resulting mixture's price becomes one ingredient
3. Repeat with the next ingredient

**Example**: Mix ₹20, ₹35, and ₹50 per kg to get ₹38/kg.
1. Mix 20 and 50: ratio = (50-38):(38-20) = 12:18 = 2:3.
   Let mixture1 = (2×20+3×50)/5 = (40+150)/5 = ₹38/kg. This gives exactly ₹38 already.
2. So just use 20 and 50 in ratio 2:3.

**For ₹38, ₹42, ₹50 to get ₹44**:
1. Mix ₹38 and ₹50 to get intermediate.
   Ratio = (50-44):(44-38) = 6:6 = 1:1. Mix amount = 1+1 = 2 parts at ₹44.
2. Mix this ₹44 with ₹42: ratio = (44-44):(44-42) = 0:2. Impossible — means you need more ₹42 to bring price down.
Use systematic approach: treat as weighted average.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Trying to apply simple two-item alligation to three items. Always reduce to a two-step process.

---

### 7. Alligation — Repeated Mixing

**Concept**: When a mixture is partially removed and replaced with another ingredient repeatedly.

**Formula for Final Concentration** (after n replacements):
```
Final = Initial × (1 - Replaced fraction)^n + Added × [1 - (1-Replaced)^n]
```

**Example**: A container has 100L of milk. 20L is removed and replaced with water. After 3 such replacements, how much milk remains?
Milk after 1 replacement = 100 × (1 - 20/100) = 80L.
After 3 replacements: 100 × (0.8)³ = 100 × 0.512 = **51.2L**

**Approximation for small replacements**: Each replacement reduces milk by r% (where r = replaced fraction).

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students simply subtract the replaced amount each time: 100 - 20 = 80, 80 - 16 = 64, 64 - 12.8 = 51.2. This happens to give the right answer for equal replacements but only because the math works out — the correct approach uses multiplication.

### 8. Ratio — Basic Concepts

**Definition**: A ratio a:b means a/b. It expresses how many times a is of b.

**Key Properties**:
- Multiply/divide both terms by same number → equivalent ratio
- a:b = ka:kb for any non-zero k
- a:b cannot be simplified if gcd(a,b) = 1

**Writing Ratios**:
- Always express in simplest form (divide by GCD)
- a:b = 2:3 means a/b = 2/3
- a:b = 3:4 means a is 3/4 of b, or a is 75% of b

**Comparison of Ratios**: To compare a:b and c:d, cross-multiply: a×d vs b×c.

**Compound Ratio**: (a:b) × (c:d) = (ac:bd)
Duplicate ratio of a:b = a²:b²
Sub-duplicate = √a:√b
Triplicate = a³:b³

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Treating ratio as difference. a:b = 3:2 means a is 50% more than b, not 150% of b.

### 9. Ratio Division — Sharing

**Concept**: Dividing a quantity in a given ratio.

**Formula**:
```
If total Q is divided in ratio a:b:c:
Share of A = Q × a / (a+b+c)
Share of B = Q × b / (a+b+c)
Share of C = Q × c / (a+b+c)
```

**Example**: ₹5,00,000 divided in ratio 3:4:8.
Sum of ratio = 3+4+8 = 15.
A = 500000 × 3/15 = **₹1,00,000**
B = 500000 × 4/15 = **₹1,33,333**
C = 500000 × 8/15 = **₹2,66,667**

> [!TIP]
> **Shortcut Method**
> Divide total by sum of ratio, then multiply by each part.

**When Individual Shares Are Given**:
If A:B = 3:4 and B:C = 5:6, find A:B:C.
Make B common: A = 3k, B = 4k.
B = 5m, C = 6m.
4k = 5m → k = 5m/4.
A = 3(5m/4) = 15m/4.
A:B:C = 15/4 : 5 : 6 = 15 : 20 : 24 = **5:4:8** after dividing by 3.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> When ratios involve multiple groups, students add them directly instead of making the common term equal first.

### 10. Direct and Inverse Proportion

**Direct Proportion**: When one increases, the other increases at the same rate.
```
a ∝ b → a/b = constant → a₁/b₁ = a₂/b₂
```

**Inverse Proportion**: When one increases, the other decreases.
```
a ∝ 1/b → ab = constant → a₁b₁ = a₂b₂
```

> [!NOTE]
> **Intuition**
> Direct = same direction. Inverse = opposite direction.

**Inverse Proportion Examples**:
- More men → less time for same work
- Higher speed → less time for same distance
- More workers → less days for same job

**Combined Proportion**:
If a ∝ b and a ∝ c, then a ∝ bc, so a = k × b × c.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Mistakes to Avoid**
> Students apply direct proportion when inverse is needed. The phrase "more workers take less time" is inverse — multiply, don't divide.

### Memory Tricks

**Alligation Visual**:
Draw a tic-tac-toe grid:
```
  Cheaper (a) -----
        |  Mean (m)  |
  Dearer (b) -----
```
The difference goes across: cheaper side gets (b-m), dearer side gets (m-a).

**Weighted Average Intuition**: The group with more members pulls the average toward its own average. Think of it as a tug-of-war where the stronger (larger) team wins.

**Ratio Sharing**: Total ÷ sum of ratio parts = one part's value. Then scale up.

**The "Devil's in the Denominator"**: For averages, always check which group is larger when combining. The larger group determines the combined average's direction.

## ⚡ Shortcut Techniques & Time-Saving Tricks

### 1. The Weighted Average — Quick Estimate

When combining two groups with averages A₁ and A₂:
- The combined average is always between A₁ and A₂
- It's closer to the average of the larger group
- Deviation of combined avg from each group = weighted by the other group's size

**Example**: 30 people avg 70, 70 people avg 50.
Combined avg = 66. Note: 66 is much closer to 50 because 70 people are pulling it down.

### 2. Alligation — The Cross Grid

```
       Dearer (D)
      /        \
 Mean (M)        Ratio = (M-a) : (D-M)
      \        /
       Cheaper (a)
```
Wait — correct grid:
```
       Dearer (D)
      /        \
 Mean (M)        Qty of cheaper = D-M
      \        /
    Cheaper (C)    Qty of dearer = M-C
```
Actually:
```
Cheaper ---- Mean ---- Dearer
   C     ---- M  ----  D
Qty of cheaper = D - M
Qty of dearer = M - C
```

### 3. Average Change — One-Liner

When average changes by ΔA by adding/removing one item:
```
Item value = Old avg + (new count × ΔA)
```
For increase: +; for decrease: -.

**Example**: Avg of 10 numbers is 40. New number raises avg to 42. Value = 40 + 11×2 = **62**

### 4. Ratio Comparison — Cross Multiply

To compare a:b and c:d:
Compare a×d with b×c.
- If a×d > b×c → a:b > c:d

**Example**: Compare 3:4 and 5:7.
3×7 = 21, 4×5 = 20. 21 > 20 → 3:4 > 5:7.

### 5. Alligation Repeated Replacement — Power Formula

After n replacements, each replacing fraction r of the mixture:
```
Fraction remaining = (1 - r)^n
Fraction replaced = 1 - (1 - r)^n
```

### 6. Mean Proportional — Square Root

Mean proportional between a and b = √(ab)
Example: Mean proportional between 4 and 9 = √36 = **6**

### 7. The "One Part" Method for Ratio Division

1. Sum all ratio parts
2. Divide total by this sum to get "one part"
3. Multiply one part by each ratio number

**Example**: 700 in ratio 3:4:5. Sum = 12. One part = 700/12 = 58.33.
Shares: 175, 233.33, 291.67. Or use fractions: 700×3/12, 700×4/12, 700×5/12.

### 8. Average of First n Natural Numbers

Average of 1,2,3,...,n = (n+1)/2
Average of first 50 numbers = 51/2 = **25.5**

### 9. Deviation from Average Method

When averaging numbers close to a reference R:
1. Calculate each deviation: dᵢ = aᵢ - R
2. Average of deviations = (sum of dᵢ)/n
3. True average = R + (avg of deviations)

This avoids adding large numbers.

### 10. Inverse Proportion Quick Check

If a is inversely proportional to b:
a₁b₁ = a₂b₂
If a doubles, b halves. If a triples, b becomes 1/3.

## ❌ Common Traps & Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Simple Average for Combined Groups
**Error**: Class A avg 70 (30 students), Class B avg 60 (70 students). Student says combined avg = (70+60)/2 = 65.
**Fix**: Use weighted average: (30×70 + 70×60)/100 = 63. The larger class pulls the average down.

### Mistake 2: Alligation Ratio Reversed
**Error**: For mixing ₹30 and ₹45 to get ₹36, student writes ratio 6:9 = 2:3, then takes more ₹30 (cheaper) than ₹45 (dearer).
**Fix**: Cheaper ingredient gets the difference from the dearer price. The dearer side is always the one farther from the mean.

### Mistake 3: Arithmetic vs Weighted Average Confusion
**Error**: When combining groups of different sizes, using (A₁+A₂)/2 instead of weighted formula.
**Fix**: Always multiply each average by its group size before adding.

### Mistake 4: Adding Ratios Directly
**Error**: A:B = 2:3, B:C = 4:5. Student says A:B:C = 2:3:5 (adding B terms).
**Fix**: Make B equal: A:B = 2:3 = 8:12. B:C = 4:5 = 12:15. So A:B:C = 8:12:15.

### Mistake 5: Confusing Direct with Inverse Proportion
**Error**: "More workers take less time" → student uses direct proportion (divides).
**Fix**: More workers, less time = inverse proportion. Multiply: workers₁ × time₁ = workers₂ × time₂.

### Mistake 6: Forgetting Group Size Change in Average
**Error**: Avg of 10 numbers is 20. New number makes avg 21. Student says new number = 20+21 = 41.
**Fix**: (10×20 + x)/11 = 21 → 200 + x = 231 → x = **31**. The group size increased by 1.

### Mistake 7: Repeated Replacement — Subtracting Instead of Multiplying
**Error**: 100L mixture, 20L replaced with water. Student: 100-20=80, 80-20=60, 60-20=40.
**Fix**: Each replacement reduces milk by a factor of (1 - 20/100) = 0.8. After 3: 100×(0.8)³ = **51.2L**.

### Mistake 8: Alligation with Mean Inside the Range
**Error**: Mixing ₹30 and ₹45 gives ₹48. Alligation impossible — mean must be between the two prices.
**Fix**: Alligation requires the mean price to be between the two ingredient prices. If ₹48 is outside 30–45, it can't be achieved with only these two ingredients.

### Mistake 9: Inverse Proportion — Wrong Direction
**Error**: If 10 workers take 20 days, 15 workers take: 10×20/15 = 13.3 days (correct for inverse proportion). But student does 10×20×15 = 3000 (wrong).
**Fix**: Use M₁D₁ = M₂D₂ for inverse proportion, not multiplication by both.

### Mistake 10: Comparing Ratios — Wrong Cross-Multiplication
**Error**: Comparing 3:5 and 2:3, student compares 3/5 vs 2/3 → 0.6 vs 0.67. Wrong because denominators differ.
**Fix**: Cross-multiply: 3×3 = 9 vs 5×2 = 10 → 2:3 > 3:5. The larger cross-product wins.

### The One Insight That Changes Everything

**Alligation is just weighted average visualized.** When you see a mixture problem, don't set up equations. Draw the cross grid: write the two prices, write the mean price in the middle, subtract diagonally. The difference on each side tells you how much of the other ingredient you need. This single visual eliminates the need for any formula in two-item mixing problems.

The second insight: **the larger group always wins in weighted average.** If you combine a large group at 60 with a small group at 40, the combined average is closer to 60. This intuition alone prevents most weighted average mistakes.
