📖 Concept Overview

What Is Profit, Loss & Discount?

At its heart, this topic is about comparing three prices:

  • Cost Price (CP): What the seller pays to acquire or produce the item
  • Selling Price (SP): What the buyer pays
  • Marked Price (MP): The price written on the tag, before discount

The difference between SP and CP determines profit or loss. The difference between MP and SP is the discount. This topic explores every possible relationship between these three numbers.

🎯 Core Concepts to Master

1. Cost Price, Selling Price, Marked Price

Definitions

  • Cost Price (CP): The actual price at which a person buys or produces an article
  • Selling Price (SP): The price at which the article is sold
  • Marked Price (MP): The price written on the article (also called List Price or Tag Price)

The Core Relationships

Profit = SP - CP (when SP > CP)
Loss = CP - SP (when CP > SP)
Discount = MP - SP
ℹ️
Intuition Think of it as a three-step transaction. The manufacturer sets a cost. The retailer adds margin to set a marked price. Then the retailer offers a discount to arrive at the selling price. Each step is a percentage of a different base.

Critical: Profit percentage is ALWAYS calculated on CP. Loss percentage is ALWAYS calculated on CP. Discount percentage is ALWAYS calculated on MP.

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students sometimes calculate profit % on SP. Always use CP as the denominator for profit/loss percentage.

2. Profit and Profit Percentage

Definition: When SP > CP, a profit is made. Profit = SP - CP

Profit Percentage: Profit expressed as a percentage of Cost Price.

Profit % = (Profit / CP) × 100
         = [(SP - CP) / CP] × 100
         = [(SP/CP) - 1] × 100

Formulas:

GivenFormula
CP and Profit %SP = CP × (1 + Profit%/100)
SP and Profit %CP = SP / (1 + Profit%/100)
CP and SPProfit % = [(SP - CP) / CP] × 100
ℹ️
Intuition If you buy something for ₹100 (CP) and sell it for ₹125 (SP), you made ₹25 profit. Profit % = 25/100 × 100 = 25%. For every ₹1 of cost, you earned ₹0.25 profit.
💡
Shortcut Method To convert profit % directly to multiplier:
Profit % → CP to SP multiplier = (100 + Profit%) / 100
  • 25% profit → SP = CP × 1.25
  • 10% profit → SP = CP × 1.10
  • 5% profit → SP = CP × 1.05
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Writing SP = CP + Profit% (e.g., ₹100 + 25% = ₹125 is correct, but ₹100 + 25 = ₹125 is coincidentally right only for ₹100). Always convert percentage to decimal before adding.

3. Loss and Loss Percentage

Definition: When CP > SP, a loss is incurred. Loss = CP - SP

Loss Percentage: Loss expressed as a percentage of Cost Price.

Loss % = (Loss / CP) × 100
        = [(CP - SP) / CP] × 100
        = [1 - (SP/CP)] × 100

Formulas:

GivenFormula
CP and Loss %SP = CP × (1 - Loss%/100)
SP and Loss %CP = SP / (1 - Loss%/100)
CP and SPLoss % = [(CP - SP) / CP] × 100
ℹ️
Intuition Loss is the mirror of profit. The formulas look identical, just with subtraction instead of addition. The multiplier is always relative to CP.

Shortcut — Key Multipliers to Memorize:

Profit/LossCP → SP Multiplier

4. Break-Even Point

Definition: The situation where SP = CP — neither profit nor loss.

Break-Even Condition: Profit % = 0% or Loss % = 0%

Finding Break-Even from Two Transactions:

If a trader sells an article at x% profit and at y% loss, and the two selling prices are equal: CP₁ × (1 + x/100) = CP₂ × (1 - y/100)

If a merchant makes x% profit on one article and y% loss on another (same CP for both): Total SP = CP + CP = 2CP → Average profit/loss = 0% (break-even)

When Same SP with Profit% and Loss%: If an article is sold at x% profit in one case and y% loss in another, and the SP is the same: CP₁ × (1 + x/100) = CP₂ × (1 - y/100) The ratio of the two CPs: CP₁/CP₂ = (1 - y/100)/(1 + x/100)

The Equal Profit and Loss Rule: If profit % = loss % (same magnitude), selling at same SP always results in: Overall = No profit, No loss only if quantities and CPs are equal. If CP is the same for both transactions and SP is the same, overall is break-even.

The Profit-Loss Cancellation Rule: When two items are sold at the same SP, one at x% profit and one at x% loss:

  • Both SP are equal = CP₁(1+x/100) = CP₂(1-x/100)
  • If CP₁ = CP₂ = CP, then SP₁ = CP(1+x/100) and SP₂ = CP(1-x/100)
  • Since SP₁ ≠ SP₂ unless x=0, "same SP" implies different CPs
  • Overall: Total SP = SP₁ + SP₂ = CP(1+x/100) + CP(1-x/100) = 2CP → Break-even

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Thinking "profit % = loss %" always means break-even. It only means break-even when the cost prices of both items are equal. If different items have different CPs, same profit% and loss% on different CPs doesn't cancel out.

5. Discount and Marked Price

Definition: Discount is the reduction from the Marked Price.

Discount Amount = MP - SP
Discount % = (Discount / MP) × 100

Key Insight: Discount is ALWAYS calculated on Marked Price (MP), not on Cost Price.

Types of Discount:

TypeDescription
Flat DiscountStraight percentage off MP
Successive DiscountTwo or more discounts applied one after another
Net DiscountEquivalent single discount for successive discounts
Formulas:
GivenFormula
MP and Discount %SP = MP × (1 - Discount%/100)
SP and Discount %MP = SP / (1 - Discount%/100)
CP, Markup %, Discount %SP = CP × (1 + Markup%/100) × (1 - Discount%/100)
ℹ️
Intuition The manufacturer sets a CP. The retailer adds a markup to get MP. Then the retailer offers a discount on MP to arrive at SP. Each step is a percentage of a different base — this is where most students get confused.

The MP → CP → SP Chain: 

CP → Markup → MP → Discount → SP
Markup is % of CP
Discount is % of MP

Example: CP = ₹100. Markup = 40%. Discount = 20%. MP = 100 × 1.40 = ₹140 SP = 140 × 0.80 = ₹112 Profit = 112 - 100 = ₹12 = 12% profit

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students calculate discount as a percentage of CP. It is always a percentage of MP. A 20% discount on MP = ₹140 means SP = ₹112, not ₹120.

6. Successive Discounts

Definition: When multiple discounts are applied one after another.

The Multiplier Method: 

Final SP = MP × (1 - d₁/100) × (1 - d₂/100) × (1 - d₃/100)

Net Discount Formula (for two discounts): 

Net Discount = d₁ + d₂ - (d₁ × d₂)/100

Example: 20% and 10% successive discounts Net = 20 + 10 - (20×10)/100 = 30 - 2 = 28% net discount MP ₹100 → SP = ₹72 (not ₹70, which would be a 30% discount — wrong!)

Why This Matters: Many students think two discounts of 20% and 10% mean 30% off. They don't. The actual discount is 28%.

The Order Myth: For successive percentage changes (discounts), the order of applying discounts does NOT change the final SP. 20% then 10% = 10% then 20% = 28% net.

Three Discounts: Net ≈ d₁ + d₂ + d₃ - (sum of pairwise products)/100 + (triple product)/10000 But easier: just chain multipliers: MP × 0.80 × 0.90 × 0.70

Exam Hack: When comparing two discount offers:

  • 20% + 10% vs 25% + 5% → compute net for both
  • 20% + 10%: MP × 0.80 × 0.90 = MP × 0.72 (28% off)
  • 25% + 5%: MP × 0.75 × 0.95 = MP × 0.7125 (28.75% off) → slightly better

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Adding discounts directly (20% + 10% = 30% off). Use the formula: 20 + 10 - 2 = 28%.

7. False Weights and Deceptive Selling

Concept: A seller uses a weight less than the claimed weight.

Example: A shopkeeper uses 900g as 1kg.

  • Claims to sell 1kg at the rate of CP per kg
  • Actually gives only 900g
  • Profit = (1000-900)/900 × 100 = 11.11%

Formula for False Weight Profit: 

If true weight = T, shown weight = S (where S < T):
Profit % = [(T - S) / S] × 100

Why This Formula: When selling at cost price but using false weight:

  • Buy T units for price of T units
  • Sell only S units but charges for T units
  • Cost of S units = (S/T) × Cost of T units
  • SP (for S units) = Full price of T units = Cost of T units
  • Profit = Cost of T units - Cost of S units = Cost of T units × (1 - S/T)
  • Profit % = Profit / Cost × 100 = (1 - S/T) / (S/T) × 100 = (T-S)/S × 100

Key Insight: The profit is calculated on the actual cost (of the S units delivered), not on the selling price.

Common False Weight Profits to Know:

Claimed WeightActual WeightProfit %
1 kg900g11.11%
1 kg800g25%
1 kg950g5.26%
1 kg750g33.33%
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students calculate profit as (T-S)/T × 100. Use S in the denominator, not T.

8. Overhead Expenses

Concept: Additional costs added to CP (transport, labour, packaging, etc.)

Effective CP = Purchase CP + Overhead Expenses
Profit/Loss calculated on Effective CP

Example: Buys article at ₹500. Transport costs ₹50. Sells at 20% profit. Effective CP = 500 + 50 = ₹550 SP = 550 × 1.20 = ₹660 Profit = 660 - 500 = ₹160 (not ₹110 which would be if transport not included)

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students forget to add overheads to CP before calculating profit percentage. Always calculate effective CP first.

9. Mixed Transactions

Concept: Selling multiple items at different profit/loss percentages.

Two Types:

Type A — Same CP, different SPs: If one article gives x% profit and another gives y% loss, both at the same CP:

  • Total CP = CP + CP = 2CP
  • Total SP = CP(1+x/100) + CP(1-y/100) = 2CP + CP(x-y)/100
  • Overall Profit/Loss = [CP(x-y)/100] / 2CP × 100 = (x-y)/2 %
  • Break-even when x = y (profit % = loss %)

Type B — Different CPs, combined profit/loss:

  • Total CP = sum of all CPs
  • Total SP = sum of all SPs
  • Overall Profit % = [(Total SP - Total CP) / Total CP] × 100

Shortcut for Equal Quantity at Different Profits: Average profit % = (sum of profit %s) / number of items (only when each item costs the same)

Example: Sells 5 items at 10% profit each, 3 items at 20% profit each. Total SP = 5 × CP × 1.10 + 3 × CP × 1.20 = 5.5CP + 3.6CP = 9.1CP Total CP = 8CP Profit % = (9.1CP - 8CP)/8CP × 100 = 1.1CP/8CP × 100 = 13.75%

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Averaging profit percentages directly. (10+20)/2 = 15% is wrong for Type B because quantities differ. Always use weighted average.

10. Equivalent Discount

Concept: Finding the single discount equivalent to multiple successive discounts.

Formula (for two discounts d₁ and d₂): 

Equivalent Discount = d₁ + d₂ - (d₁ × d₂)/100

For three discounts: Apply formula iteratively, or use multipliers directly.

Example: Find equivalent discount for 20%, 10%, and 5%. MP × 0.80 × 0.90 × 0.95 = MP × 0.684 Equivalent discount = 100 - 68.4 = 31.6%

Use Case: Comparing two "buy X get Y free" offers, or comparing discount offers from different shops.

11. Partnership and Cost Sharing

Concept: When two or more people invest in a business and share profits/losses in ratio of their investments.

Profit Sharing Ratio = Capital × Time If A invests ₹X for T years and B invests ₹Y for the same period, profit sharing ratio = X:Y

If investments are for different periods: Effective capital = Capital × Time A's share: X₁ × T₁, B's share: X₂ × T₂

Example: A invests ₹5000 for 6 months, B invests ₹8000 for 4 months. A's contribution-months = 5000 × 6 = 30000 B's contribution-months = 8000 × 4 = 32000 Ratio = 30000:32000 = 15:16

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Forgetting to multiply by time. Equal capital for equal time → equal profit share. But equal capital for different time → different share.

⚡ Shortcut Techniques & Time-Saving Tricks

1. The Percentage Equivalence Method

Convert profit/loss percentages to their fraction form for faster calculation:

Profit %MultiplierFractionWhen Useful
100%2.002×When you double money
50%1.503/2Half again
25%1.255/4Quarter more
20%1.206/5Fifth more
10%1.1011/10Tenth more
Example: Sells at 25% profit. CP = ₹240. SP = 240 × 1.25 = 240 × 5/4 = 300. (Calculated as 240÷4=60, ×5=300)

2. The Cross-Multiplication Ratio Method

When SP is same but CP differs: If an article gives 20% profit when CP = ₹A, and x% loss when CP = ₹B, and SP is same: A × 1.20 = B × (1 - x/100)

When CP is same but SP differs: Find the percentage difference between SPs directly.

3. Discount Comparison Shortcut

Comparing d₁%+d₂% vs d₃%+d₄%: Just compute (100-d₁)(100-d₂) vs (100-d₃)(100-d₄) Larger product = better for customer (more off).

Example: 20%+10% vs 15%+15%?

  • 80×90 = 7200
  • 85×85 = 7225 → second offer is slightly better (28% vs 27.75%)

4. The Gain = Loss Cancellation Method

When a merchant sells at x% profit on some items and x% loss on others (same CP for all):

  • Total SP = 2 × CP (break-even)
  • Only if all items have the same CP

If different CPs: Use Type B mixed transaction method.

5. Profit on Selling Price vs Cost Price

Always express profit/loss as % of CP, not SP. If asked for "profit as % of SP", use: Profit % of SP = (Profit / SP) × 100 This is a different number from Profit % of CP.

Example: CP=100, SP=125

  • Profit % of CP = 25%
  • Profit % of SP = 25/125 × 100 = 20%

Common in retail: "Our margin is 20%" often means 20% of SP, not CP. Know the context.

6. The "What should be the SP" Shortcut

To achieve a target profit % after offering d% discount on MP:

  1. Assume MP = 100
  2. After discount d%: SP = 100 - d = (100 - d)
  3. To get target profit t%: CP × (1 + t/100) = SP = 100 - d
  4. MP = 100 × (1 + t/100) / (1 - d/100)

8. The "Replacement" Trick

When one item is replaced by another in a transaction:

  • Track the CP of original, then the CP of replacement
  • Apply profit/loss percentage to the new CP
  • Compare final SP with original CP or SP

❌ Common Traps & Mistakes

Mistake 1: Profit % on SP Instead of CP

Error: CP=100, SP=125. Student says profit% = 25/125 × 100 = 20%. Reality: Profit % = 25/100 × 100 = 25%. Always use CP as denominator. Fix: In P&L questions, "profit %" always means % of CP. Only change denominator if explicitly asked "profit as % of SP."

Mistake 2: Adding Discounts Directly

Error: Two discounts of 20% and 10% → total = 30% off (wrong!) Fix: MP × 0.80 × 0.90 = MP × 0.72 → 28% total discount. Use net discount formula: d₁+d₂-(d₁×d₂)/100.

Mistake 3: Discount Applied on CP Instead of MP

Error: CP=100, markup 40%, discount 20%. SP = 100 × 0.80 = 80 (wrong!) Fix: MP = 100 × 1.40 = 140. SP = 140 × 0.80 = 112. Always apply discount on MP, not CP.

Mistake 4: Profit % = Loss % Always Means Break-Even

Error: Selling two items at 20% profit and 20% loss → break-even. Fix: This is only true when both items have the same CP. With different CPs, same % profit and loss does NOT cancel out.

Mistake 5: Ignoring Overhead Expenses

Error: CP=500, overheads=50, profit 20%. SP = 500 × 1.20 = 600. Fix: Effective CP = 500+50 = 550. SP = 550 × 1.20 = 660. Profit = 660-500 = 160 (or 32% on purchase price).

Mistake 6: Using Wrong Multiplier for Discount Reverse

Error: MP=500, discount 20%. MP = 500/0.80 = 625 (this is correct actually) But: SP=400, discount 20%. Finding MP: student uses 400 × 0.80 = 320 (wrong!) Fix: MP = SP / (1 - d/100) = 400 / 0.80 = 500. Multiply to go from MP to SP, divide to go back.

Mistake 7: False Weight Profit — Wrong Denominator

Error: True 1000g, shown 900g. Profit = (1000-900)/1000 × 100 = 10%. Fix: Profit % = (T-S)/S × 100 = (1000-900)/900 × 100 = 11.11%. Denominator is S (actual amount sold), not T.

Mistake 8: Forgetting the 25% + 20% = Break-Even Rule

Error: A shop marks at 25% above cost and gives 20% discount → thinks there's profit. Fix: 25% markup + 20% discount = 1.25 × 0.80 = 1.00 = break-even. No profit, no loss.

Mistake 9: Mixing Up Markup and Margin

Error: Cost=100, markup 50% → SP=150. Then says margin is 50% (it's 33.33% actually). Fix: Markup % = (Markup Amount / CP) × 100. Margin % = (Markup Amount / SP) × 100. For the same values, margin < markup.

Mistake 10: Chain Discount — Wrong Order Assumption

Error: Believes 20% then 10% discount gives different result than 10% then 20%. Fix: Multiplication is commutative. 0.80 × 0.90 = 0.90 × 0.80. Order doesn't matter for successive discounts.

  1. A dealer buys goods at a 20% discount on MP. He wants to make a profit of 25% on CP after offering a discount of d% on MP. He also has to pay a commission of 5% on the SP. Find the value of d.

The One Insight That Changes Everything

Discount % is always on MP. Profit % is always on CP.

Keep that separation crystal clear in your head and you'll never get the chain problems wrong. A 40% markup on CP followed by a 30% discount on MP does NOT result in any predictable profit or loss — you have to chain the multipliers: CP × 1.40 × 0.70 = SP. That's the single line that solves every chain problem in this topic.
