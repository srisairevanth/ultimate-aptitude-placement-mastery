📖 Concept Overview

What Are Percentages?

A percentage is simply a fraction with a denominator of 100. The word "percent" comes from Latin per centum — "by the hundred." So 45% means 45 out of every 100 parts. The Core Insight: Everything in percentages reduces to one simple idea — work with base 100, then scale.

2. Finding Percentage of a Number

Formula: x% of N = (x/100) × N
ℹ️
Intuition Move the decimal two places left for 1%, then multiply.
Example: 15% of 240 Method 1: (15/100) × 240 = 0.15 × 240 = 36 Method 2 (fast): 10% = 24, 5% = 12, 15% = 24 + 12 = 36 Method 3 (fastest): 15% = 3/20 → 240/20 × 3 = 12 × 3 = 36 Shortcut — The 10% Split: Break any percentage into multiples of 10% and 5%, which are easy to calculate mentally.
  • 35% = 3×10% + 5%
  • 27% = 2×10% + 5% + 2×1%
  • 15% = 10% + 5%
The Commutative Trick (most powerful shortcut): x% of y = y% of x This means you can swap to whichever is easier to calculate. Examples:
  • 18% of 50 = 50% of 18 = 9 ✓
  • 8% of 25 = 25% of 8 = 2 ✓
  • 4% of 75 = 75% of 4 = 3 ✓
  • 6% of 150 = 150% of 6 = 9 ✓
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students calculate 4% of 75 by doing 75 × 0.04 = 3.0. When they could instantly see 75% of 4 = 3.

3. Finding What Percentage One Number Is of Another

Formula: (Part / Whole) × 100 = Percentage
ℹ️
Intuition Always divide the smaller by the larger when finding "what percent of A is B?" The base (A) goes in the denominator.
Standard Phrase Translation:
PhraseWhat It Means
"A is what % of B?"(A / B) × 100
"What % of A is B?"(B / A) × 100
"A is x% more than B"A = B × (1 + x/100)
"A is x% less than B"A = B × (1 - x/100)
"% increase from A to B"[(B-A)/A] × 100
"% decrease from A to B"[(A-B)/A] × 100
Example: If the price of a shirt goes from ₹400 to ₹500, by what percent did it increase? % increase = (500-400)/400 × 100 = 100/400 × 100 = 25%
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Using the new value as the denominator instead of the original. % increase always has the original value as base.

4. Percentage Increase & Decrease

Increase Formula: New = Original × (1 + r/100) Decrease Formula: New = Original × (1 - r/100)
ℹ️
Intuition Think of it like a multiplier. 1.25 means 125% = 25% increase from base.
Key Relationship: If A increases by r%, it becomes A(1 + r/100). If it then decreases by r%, it does NOT return to original. Example: ₹100 → 20% increase → ₹120 → 20% decrease → ₹96. Not ₹100. The Symmetry Trick:
  • If something increases by x%, to find the original: divide by (1 + x/100)
  • If something decreases by x%, to find the original: divide by (1 - x/100)
Example: A car costs ₹5,00,000 after a 25% increase. Original price? Original = 5,00,000 / 1.25 = ₹4,00,000
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students multiply by (1 - r/100) when finding original after decrease. Should divide, not multiply.

5. Successive Percentage Change (The Multiplier Method)

Concept: When multiple percentage changes happen one after another, use multiplication of multipliers, not addition. Formula: Final = Original × (1 + r₁/100) × (1 + r₂/100) × ... Example: A product's price is increased by 20%, then decreased by 10%. Final = 100 × 1.20 × 0.90 = 108 Net % change = 8% increase Shortcuts for Common Combinations:
  • Increase by x%, then decrease by x%: Net change = -(x²/100)%
Example: +20%, -20% → -400/100 = -4% (as shown above)
  • Increase by a% and b%: Net ≈ a + b + (ab/100)%
  • Decrease by a% and b%: Net ≈ -(a + b) + (ab/100)%
  • Increase by a%, decrease by b%: Net = a - b - (ab/100)%
The +a%, -b% Net Formula: Net % change = a - b - (a × b)/100 Example: Price increases by 30%, then decreases by 20%. Net = 30 - 20 - (30×20)/100 = 10 - 6 = 4% increase Exam Hack: When a value changes by x% and y% successively, the order matters for perception but the multiplier method always gives the correct final value.
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Adding percentages directly. 20% increase followed by 20% decrease is NOT 0% — it's a 4% net decrease.

6. Reverse Percentage (Finding the Original)

Concept: Given the result after a percentage change, find the original value. Formula: Original = New Value / (1 ± r/100) Two Types: Type 1 — After Increase: Original = New / (1 + r/100) Example: After 25% increase, price = ₹625. Original = 625/1.25 = ₹500 Type 2 — After Decrease: Original = New / (1 - r/100) Example: After 20% discount, price = ₹400. Original = 400/0.80 = ₹500 The Universal Reverse Formula: Original × (1 ± r/100) = New → Original = New / (1 ± r/100) → Multiply numerator and denominator by 100 to clear decimals. Example: 37.5% of a number is 225. Find the number. 37.5% = 3/8 (3/8) × N = 225 → N = 225 × 8/3 = 600
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Forgetting to use the reciprocal multiplier. After a 20% decrease, multiplying by 0.8 gives new, not original. Must divide by 0.8.

7. Percentage Points vs Percentage

Concept: Percentage points measure absolute difference between two percentages. Percentage measures relative change.
ℹ️
Intuition If interest rate goes from 8% to 10%, it increased by 2 percentage points but increased by 25% relative to the original rate.
Formula:
  • Percentage point difference = |Rate₁ - Rate₂|
  • Percentage change in rate = [(Rate₂ - Rate₁) / Rate₁] × 100
Example: Old price: ₹100, New price: ₹150
  • Price increased by 50% (percentage)
  • Price increased by 50 percentage points? No — wrong use.
Real-World Use:
  • RBI cuts repo rate by 50 basis points = 0.5 percentage points
  • "Inflation fell from 8% to 6%" → fell by 2 percentage points, fell by 25% relative
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students confuse "percentage point change" with "percentage change." This is the #1 trap in economic data interpretation questions.

8. Population/Growth Problems

Formula:
  • After n years at r% per annum: P × (1 + r/100)^n
  • For decrease: P × (1 - r/100)^n
The Average Growth Rate: If population grows from P to Q in n years: r = [(Q/P)^(1/n) - 1] × 100 Example: Population grows from 10,000 to 13,310 in 3 years. Find rate. 13000 = 10000 × (1 + r/100)^3 1.33 = (1 + r/100)^3 1 + r/100 = 1.1 → r = 10% Half-Life / Doubling Time (Advanced):
  • Doubling time at r%: T = 72/r years (Rule of 72)
  • For r=8%: doubles in 9 years (72/8 = 9)
Example: At 12% per annum, population of a town doubles every 6 years. 2 years ago it was 40,000. What is it now? Now = 40000 × (1.12)^2 = 40000 × 1.2544 = 50,176
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Using simple percentage growth formula for compound growth problems. Always use (1 + r/100)^n.

9. Market Share / Election Problems

Concept: Share percentages of parts that together form a whole. Formula:
  • Share of A = (A's value / Total) × 100%
  • New share after change: recalculate total
Example: Total votes = 50,000. Candidate A gets 35%. After losing 20% of their votes to B, what are new shares? A's votes = 17500. Loses 20%: loses 3500 to B. New A = 14000, New total = 50000 (unchanged, just transferred) New A% = 14000/50000 × 100 = 28% Shortcut for Share Changes: If A loses x% of its value to B:
  • New A% = (A% - x% × A% / 100)
  • New B% = (B% + x% × A% / 100)
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students forget that when A loses votes to B, the total stays the same. They incorrectly adjust the total.

10. Discount and Marked Price

Concept: Three prices — Cost Price (CP), Marked Price (MP), Selling Price (SP). Formulas:
  • Discount % = [(MP - SP) / MP] × 100
  • Profit % = [(SP - CP) / CP] × 100
  • Loss % = [(CP - SP) / CP] × 100
The Discount Chain: If two successive discounts of a% and b% are offered: Net discount = a + b - (ab/100)% Example: 20% and 10% off → 20 + 10 - (200/100) = 28% total discount Comparison Trick:
  • "30% off on ₹500" vs "20% off on ₹400": 30% of 500 = 150 off. 20% of 400 = 80 off. First is better.
When MP is Given as Percentage Above CP: If MP is marked at x% above CP and a discount of d% is given: SP = CP × (1 + x/100) × (1 - d/100) Profit % = (SP - CP)/CP × 100 Example: Cost = ₹200. Marked at 40% above cost. Sold at 20% discount. MP = 200 × 1.40 = 280 SP = 280 × 0.80 = 224 Profit = (224 - 200)/200 × 100 = 12%
⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid Students calculate SP directly as CP + x% - d% (e.g., CP + 40% - 20% = 20% profit). This is wrong because the discount applies on MP, not CP.

Memory Tricks

The "Fraction-First" Trick: Convert percentages to fractions mentally
  • 75% = 3/4 → always round to 3/4 of the number
  • 125% = 5/4 → the number plus 1/4 of itself
  • 150% = 3/2 → the number plus half of itself
The "Near 100" Trick: For percentages near 100, use (100% ± small%)
  • 98% of 250 = (100% - 2%) of 250 = 250 - 5 = 245
  • 103% of 250 = (100% + 3%) of 250 = 250 + 7.5 = 257.5

⚡ Shortcut Techniques & Time-Saving Tricks

1. The 10% Mental Calculation Method

For any percentage, decompose into 10%s and 5%s: Example: 35% of 480
  • 10% of 480 = 48
  • 5% of 480 = 24
  • 35% = 3×10% + 5% + 5% (no, 3×10% = 30%, need 5% more)
  • 35% = 48 × 3 + 24 = 144 + 24 = 168
For 37.5%: Use 3/8 directly = 480 ÷ 8 × 3 = 60 × 3 = 180

2. The Swap Trick (Most Powerful)

x% of y = y% of x Always calculate the easier side.
CalculationSwap ToResult

4% of 7575% of 43

3. Base-100 Method

Convert everything to base 100, then scale: Example: If 35% of students passed and 455 passed, how many failed? 35% passed → 35 out of 100 455 passed → each "unit" = 455/35 = 13 Total students = 100 × 13 = 1300 Failed = 1300 - 455 = 845

4. The Difference Method for Percentage Comparison

To find what percent A is of B without division:
  • A is what percent of B? → Cross-multiply by a convenient percentage
  • Is 24 greater or less than 30% of 80? (30% of 80 = 24) → exactly 30%

5. Successive Change Shortcuts

Increase by x%, then decrease by y%: Net = (x - y - xy/100)% Special Cases:
  • +x%, -x%: Net change = -(x²/100)%
  • +50%, -50%: Net = 0 - 2500/100 = -25% (halves!)
  • +20%, -10%: Net = 20 - 10 - 2 = 8% increase
Three successive changes: Net = r₁ + r₂ + r₃ + (r₁r₂ + r₂r₃ + r₃r₁)/100 + (r₁r₂r₃)/10000 (But usually just chain the multipliers: 1.20 × 0.90 × 1.10)

6. The 100 minus Method

When finding what percentage does NOT have a certain property:
  • If 40% passed, then 60% failed
  • If 15% are absent, then 85% are present
  • If 30% like product A, then up to 70% don't

7. Approximate Percentage from Fraction

Convert difficult percentages to nearby simple fractions:
  • 33% ≈ 1/3 → use when you see "approximately one-third"
  • 66% ≈ 2/3 → double one-third
  • 14% ≈ 1/7 → useful for share problems

🔥 Most Frequently Asked Question Patterns

Pattern 1: Find Percentage of a Number

Concept: Basic percentage calculation Difficulty: Easy Approach: x% of N = (x/100) × N or decompose into 10% + 5% Fastest: Use commutative swap if applicable Companies: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant, Accenture

Pattern 2: Percentage Increase / Decrease

Concept: Finding % change from old to new Difficulty: Easy Approach: (Change / Original) × 100 Fastest: Remember to always use original as base Companies: All companies

Pattern 3: Successive Percentage Change

Concept: Two or more percentage changes applied Difficulty: Medium Approach: Multiply multipliers, or use net formula Fastest: (1 + r₁/100)(1 + r₂/100)... = final multiplier Companies: TCS, Infosys, Amazon, Goldman

Pattern 4: Reverse Percentage (Original Value)

Concept: Given final value after change, find original Difficulty: Medium Approach: Divide by multiplier for increase; divide by (1-r/100) for decrease Fastest: Reciprocal method Companies: TCS, Infosys, Deloitte, Amazon

Pattern 5: Population / Compound Growth

Concept: P × (1 + r/100)^n Difficulty: Medium–Hard Approach: Use formula, or for small years chain multipliers Fastest: For doubling/halving: Rule of 72 Companies: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman Sachs, Amazon

Pattern 6: Discount Problems (Single or Chain)

Concept: Successive discounts on marked price Difficulty: Easy–Medium Approach: Net discount = a + b - ab/100 Fastest: MP × (1 - d₁/100) × (1 - d₂/100) Companies: All companies

Pattern 7: Profit & Loss as Percentage

Concept: SP, CP, markup, discount chain Difficulty: Medium Approach: SP = CP × (1 + profit%) = MP × (1 - discount%) Fastest: Work backwards from known values Companies: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant, Deloitte

Pattern 8: Finding What Percent of A is B

Concept: Relative percentage Difficulty: Easy Approach: (B/A) × 100 — always divide smaller by larger for "what percent of" Fastest: Cross-check with 50%, 25% benchmarks Companies: All companies

Pattern 9: Percentage Points vs Percentage

Concept: Interest rate, inflation, market share changes Difficulty: Medium Approach: Separate absolute (pp) from relative (%) Fastest: Identify the phrase "percentage points" Companies: Deloitte, Goldman, Amazon, Microsoft

Pattern 10: Share Redistribution

Concept: When one party's share changes, redistributing to others Difficulty: Medium Approach: Calculate absolute values first, then new percentages Fastest: Keep total constant, recalculate Companies: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman

Pattern 11: Finding New Percentage After Change

Concept: Base changes, then find new percentage Difficulty: Medium–Hard Approach: Convert to absolute values, adjust, reconvert to percentage Fastest: Never try to adjust the percentage directly — go to numbers first Companies: Infosys, Amazon, Goldman

Pattern 12: Expressing Fraction as Percentage

Concept: Given a fraction, express as percentage Difficulty: Easy Approach: Multiply fraction by 100 Fastest: Know 1/3=33.33%, 1/6=16.67%, 1/7=14.29%, 1/8=12.5% Companies: All companies

Pattern 13: Error / Approximation in Percentage

Concept: Approximate value, find error % Difficulty: Medium Approach: % error = [(Approx - Exact) / Exact] × 100 Fastest: Use percentage of exact value Companies: Deloitte, Amazon, Microsoft

Pattern 14: Comparison Using Percentage

Concept: Which is greater — A's x% or B's y%? Difficulty: Easy–Medium Approach: Calculate both in absolute terms, or use ratio Fastest: Cross-multiply: Is (x% of A) > (y% of B)? → Is (x × A) > (y × B)? Companies: TCS, Infosys, Cognizant

Pattern 15: Percentage Above/Below a Value

Concept: "A is 25% more than B" → A = 1.25B Difficulty: Easy Approach: More than → multiply by (1 + r/100); Less than → multiply by (1 - r/100) Fastest: Direct formula Companies: All companies

Pattern 16: Two Successive Changes — Find Original

Concept: Given final value after +a% then -b%, find original Difficulty: Hard Approach: Original = Final / [(1 + a/100)(1 - b/100)] Fastest: Work backwards using reciprocal multipliers Companies: Amazon, Goldman, Microsoft

Pattern 17: Mixing Two Quantities at Different Percentages

Concept: Alligation at percentage level Difficulty: Medium–Hard Approach: Weighted average percentage = (sum of values × percentages) / total Fastest: Weighted average formula Companies: Deloitte, Goldman, Amazon

Pattern 18: Time-Value Percentage Problems

Concept: Depreciation, appreciation over time Difficulty: Medium Approach: Use (1 ± r/100)^n, or for depreciation: (1 - r/100)^n Fastest: Rule of 72 for doubling/halving times Companies: Infosys, Deloitte, Goldman

Pattern 19: What Should the New Percentage Be?

Concept: "By what percent must sales increase to achieve target?" Difficulty: Medium Approach: (Target - Current) / Current × 100 Fastest: Direct formula Companies: Infosys, Deloitte, Amazon

Pattern 20: Percentage in Pie Chart DI

Concept: Extract percentage from visual, apply to total Difficulty: Easy–Medium Approach: Read off percentage, apply to total value Fastest: Cross-multiply to verify percentages sum to 100 Companies: All companies (DI section)

Phase 1: Fundamentals (Days 1-3)

  3. Master finding % of a number (decompose into 10% + 5%)
  4. Learn % increase/decrease formula with original as base
  5. Practice: 50 basic percentage-of-number questions
  6. Target: Solve basic % questions in under 15 seconds

Phase 2: Intermediate Concepts (Days 4-7)

  1. Successive percentage changes (multiplier method)
  2. Two-discount chain formula
  3. Reverse percentage — finding original
  4. Percentage points vs percentage difference
  5. Practice: 50 intermediate questions
  6. Target: Solve successive change problems in under 30 seconds

Phase 3: Advanced Concepts (Days 8-12)

  1. Multi-year compound growth/population problems
  2. Three or more successive changes
  3. Share redistribution problems
  4. Mixed percentage + ratio problems
  5. Practice: 50 advanced questions
  6. Target: Solve compound growth problems in under 45 seconds

Phase 4: Placement-Level Practice (Days 13-18)

  1. Solve 200+ previous year questions (TCS, Infosys, Cognizant)
  2. Take timed section tests (10 questions in 10 minutes)
  3. Review mistakes — categorize as conceptual, calculation, or reading error
  4. Focus on DI-based percentage questions
  5. Target: 85%+ accuracy, 1 question per 50 seconds

Phase 5: Product-Based Company Level (Days 19-25)

  1. Solve Goldman Sachs and Amazon-specific percentage problems
  2. Practice "what should the new percentage be" reverse problems
  3. Handle multi-step percentage problems combining 3+ concepts
  4. Practice DI sets where percentages drive the entire set
  5. Target: 75%+ accuracy at hard level

❌ Common Traps & Mistakes

Mistake 1: Using New Value as Base for % Change

Error: Saying price increased 50% when it went from ₹200 to ₹300 (should use ₹200 as base) Fix: Always identify the original value first. % change = (Change/Original) × 100.

Mistake 2: Adding Percentages Directly for Successive Changes

Error: 20% increase then 20% decrease = 0% change (wrong!) Fix: Use multipliers: 1.20 × 0.80 = 0.96 = 4% decrease.

Mistake 3: Confusing Percentage Points with Percentage

Error: "Inflation fell 2% from 8%" → thinking it's 2% relative drop (it's actually 25% relative drop, or 2 percentage points) Fix: When you see "percentage points," write down the raw difference. When you see "% change in rate," calculate relative change.

Mistake 4: Multiplying Instead of Dividing in Reverse Percentage

Error: After 20% decrease, price is ₹400. Original = 400 × 0.80 = ₹320 (wrong!) Fix: Original = 400 / 0.80 = ₹500. Divide to go backward.

Mistake 5: Applying Discount on Cost Price Instead of Marked Price

Error: Cost ₹200, marked at 40% above CP, 20% discount. SP = 200 × 0.80 = ₹160 (wrong!) Fix: MP = 200 × 1.40 = 280. SP = 280 × 0.80 = ₹224. Discount always applies on MP.

Mistake 6: Forgetting That 0% and 100%+ Are Valid

Error: Thinking percentages can only range from 0% to 100% Fix: 150% means 1.5 times original. 0% means zero. Both are valid outcomes.

Mistake 7: Mixing Up "More Than" and "Less Than" Directions

Error: "A is 20% less than B" → writing A = B × 1.20 (should be 0.80) Fix: "More than" → add (1 + r/100). "Less than" → subtract (1 - r/100).

Mistake 8: Not Checking Answer in Context

Error: Getting 150% of 80 = 160, and saying it's 160% of 80 (correct) but then confusing the answer format Fix: Always verify: 150% of 80 = 1.5 × 80 = 120. The 120 is the new value, 150 is the percentage.

Mistake 9: Approximating Too Aggressively

Error: 33.3% ≈ 33% when the result should be exact Fix: Know when exact is needed. In exam approximations, note the direction of error.

Mistake 10: Chain Discount Calculation — Adding Instead of Formula

Error: Two discounts of 30% and 20% → total discount = 50% (wrong — actual is 44%) Fix: Net discount = 30 + 20 - (30×20)/100 = 50 - 6 = 44%. Or: SP = MP × 0.70 × 0.80 = MP × 0.56 = 44% off.

🔍 View Solution 37.5% of 160 37.5% = 3/8 → 160/8 × 3 = 20 × 3 = 60

🔴 Hard Questions (1-20)

  1. The price of a stock increases by 20% on Monday, decreases by 15% on Tuesday, and increases by 10% on Wednesday. If it started at ₹100, what is the final price?
🔍 View Solution +20%, -15%, +10% from 100 100 × 1.20 × 0.85 × 1.10 = 100 × (1.20 × 0.85) × 1.10 = 100 × 1.02 × 1.10 = 100 × 1.122 = ₹112.20
  1. A shopkeeper sells an article at a profit of 25%. If he had bought it at 20% less and sold it at ₹20 less, he would have made a profit of 30%. Find the cost price.
🔍 View Solution Let CP = x. SP = 1.25x. If bought at 20% less: 0.80x. Sold at 1.25x - 20. Profit = (1.25x - 20 - 0.80x) / 0.80x × 100 = 30% (0.45x - 20) / 0.80x = 0.30 0.45x - 20 = 0.24x → 0.21x = 20 → x = ₹95.24 (approximately)
  1. The population of a village increases by 10% per year. In what time will it double?
🔍 View Solution Doubling at 10% per year Use Rule of 72: T ≈ 72/10 = 7.2 years Exact: (1.10)^n = 2 → n × log(1.10) = log(2) → n = 0.3010/0.0414 ≈ 7.27
  1. If A's income is 20% less than B's, by what percent is B's income more than A's?
🔍 View Solution A = 1.20B B = A/1.20 = 0.833A B is 16.67% less than A. % less = (A - B)/A × 100 = (1 - 0.833)/1 × 100 = 16.67% Formula: If A is x% more than B, B is [x/(100+x)] × 100 less than A.
  1. A mixture contains milk and water in the ratio 3:2. If 10 litres of water is added, the ratio becomes 3:4. How much milk was in the original mixture?
🔍 View Solution Let milk = 3k, water = 2k. After adding 10L water: milk:water = 3k:(2k+10) = 3:4 3k / (2k+10) = 3/4 → 12k = 3(2k+10) → 12k = 6k + 30 → 6k = 30 → k = 5 Milk = 15 litres
  1. The price of a commodity first increases by x%, then decreases by x%. The final price is 96% of the original. Find x.
🔍 View Solution +x%, -x% → final = 96% (1 + x/100)(1 - x/100) = 0.96 1 - (x²/10000) = 0.96 x²/10000 = 0.04 → x² = 400 → x = 20
  1. In an examination, 40% students failed in Physics, 30% failed in Chemistry, and 30% failed in both. What percentage passed in both subjects?
🔍 View Solution Failed Physics: 40%, Failed Chemistry: 30%, Failed Both: 30% Passed in Physics = 60%, Passed in Chemistry = 70% Passed in both = 60% + 70% - 100% = 30% (inclusion-exclusion)
  1. A's salary is 50% more than B's. B's salary is what percent less than A's?
🔍 View Solution A = 1.50B B/A = 1/1.50 = 2/3 = 0.667 B is 33.33% of A → B is 33.33% less than A
  1. If the side of a cube increases by 10%, by what percent does the volume increase?
🔍 View Solution Side ↑ 10%, volume change Volume ∝ side³ New volume = (1.10)³ = 1.331 Increase = 33.1%
  1. The value of a machine depreciates at 20% per year. After how many years will its value be 40% of the original?

  1. In an election, 75% of voters voted. The winning candidate got 55% of valid votes and won by a margin of 4,200 votes. Find the total number of voters.

  1. The population of a town is 20,000. It increases at 10% for the first year and 20% for the second year. In the third year, it decreases by 10%. What is the population at the end of 3 years?

  1. The population of a village was 10,000 in 2010. It grew at 8% per year. In what year will it cross 25,000?

  1. An item is sold at a profit of 20%. If the cost price is reduced by 15% and selling price remains the same, what is the new profit percentage?
🔍 View Solution Original: CP = x, SP = 1.20x New CP = 0.85x, SP unchanged = 1.20x New profit = (1.20x - 0.85x) / 0.85x × 100 = 0.35x/0.85x × 100 = 41.18%
  1. A tank is 80% full. After removing 40 litres, it becomes 60% full. The tank's full capacity is what percent more than the current water amount?
🔍 View Solution Full capacity = C. 80% full → 0.80C. Remove 40L → 60% full → 0.60C. 40L = 0.20C → C = 200L Current water = 0.80C = 160L Full capacity is 200L, current is 160L. % more = (200-160)/160 × 100 = 40/160 × 100 = 25% more
  1. A man buys goods worth ₹10,000. He sells half at a profit of 20% and the rest at a loss of 10%. At what percent profit or loss must he sell the remaining goods to achieve an overall profit of 10%?
🔍 View Solution Total cost = 10000. Half at 20% profit, half at 10% loss. Half goods: cost = 5000, SP = 5000 × 1.20 = 6000 Other half: cost = 5000, SP = 5000 × 0.90 = 4500 Total SP so far = 10500 Need 10% overall profit on 10000: Total SP needed = 11000 Remaining goods cost = 5000. Need SP = 11000 - 10500 = 500. % profit on remaining = (500-5000)/5000 × 100 = -80% He needs to sell at a 80% loss on the remaining goods — which means he needs to reconsider his strategy or the problem implies he can sell remaining at a higher price.
  1. The side of a square is increased by 5% and then decreased by 5%. The area of the resulting figure is what percent less than the original area?
🔍 View Solution Side +5% then -5% New side = 1.05 × 0.95 × s = 0.9975s ≈ 99.75% of original Area = (0.9975)² = 0.9950 → 0.5% decrease
  1. In an examination, 60% passed in Mathematics, 50% passed in Physics, and 40% passed in both. 500 students failed in both subjects. Find the total number of students.
🔍 View Solution Passed in Math = 60%, Passed in Physics = 50%, Both = 40% Passed in at least one: 60+50-40 = 70% Failed in both = 30% = 500 students Total students = 500/0.30 = 1666.67 ≈ 1667 (round to whole)
  1. A sum of money becomes 3 times itself in 5 years at simple interest. What is the rate of interest? What would it be at compound interest?
🔍 View Solution Simple Interest: sum becomes 3× in 5 years SI = 3P - P = 2P. Rate = (2P × 100)/(P × 5) = 40% per annum (very high) Compound Interest: Find rate where (1+r)^5 = 3 r = 3^(1/5) - 1 = 1.2457 - 1 = 0.2457 = 24.57% per annum
